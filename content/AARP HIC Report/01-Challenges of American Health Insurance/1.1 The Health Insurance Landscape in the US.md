# The Health Insurance Landscape in the US

Health insurance in the United States is a complex and disjointed system comprised of both public and private insurance coverage options. Aging adults must manage these intricacies in addition to navigating specific challenges related to their age and life transitions. General barriers in the US healthcare and insurance systems combine with barriers related to aging adults' unique background and social position to create compounding access issues. These distinct and additive barriers can be addressed both by understanding the general landscape of health insurance coverage in the US that aging adults must navigate through, while also recognizing their particular circumstances, needs, and experiences. In this report, we will first cover general information on health insurance coverage options that aging adults interface with, before exploring insurance complexities unique to, or amplified for, older adults.

## National Coverage Rates

 In 2023, the US Census Bureau reported that about **92% of people in the United States were covered by health insurance**.  

> [!QUOTE] [Keisler-Starkey, Katherine, and Lisa N Bunch. 2024. _Health Insurance Coverage in the United States: 2023_. Nos. P60-284. Current Population Reports. United States Census Bureau.](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/Y2GI2RYU)
> "Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance ([[Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance (ESI)|ESI]]) was the most common type of coverage, "covering 53.7 percent of the population for some or all of the calendar year, followed by Medicaid (18.9 percent), Medicare (18.9 percent), direct-purchase coverage (10.2 percent), TRICARE (2.6 percent), and VA and CHAMPVA coverage (1.0 percent)."

Since the US [[Healthcare]] system does not operate on a single payer system, significant disparities in health coverage exist in the US across demographic groups[^1] and by type of insurance coverage. The four most common types of insurance are [[Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance (ESI)]], [[Medicaid]], [[Medicare]], and private insurance.

A major piece of legislation that reformed the US health insurance landscape was the [[Affordable Care Act]] (ACA). The ACA was enacted in 2010 to broaden access to affordable health insurance for low-income populations and had three major impacts: introducing regulations for the private insurance market, creating healthcare [[ACA Marketplace|marketplaces]], and expanding Medicaid coverage[^2]. These reforms are particularly important for aging adults who are facing unstable insurance coverage, such as from retiring early, losing employer-sponsored coverage, or income fluctuations before becoming eligible for Medicare at age 65. 

For many adults 40-65, the ACA has become a critical bridge to continuous, affordable health coverage in a life stage when managing chronic conditions and accessing [[Preventive Care]] becomes increasingly important. The ACA mandates that insurance plans cover essential health services, like preventative and primary care, and prohibits insurance companies from discriminating against enrollees, such as by offering different rates or denying coverage to people based on gender or preexisting conditions. 

Additionally, the restrictions limit how much insurers can increase rates based on age. The Act also introduced limits on out-of-pocket costs for in-network care and required preventative care to be covered on plans[^3]. **The ACA helps ensure that aging adults can obtain and maintain private insurance coverage without facing excessive costs or denial of care due to their age or health status or diagnoses.**

### Private Insurance

In the United States, privately purchased insurance for adults is an essential component of the healthcare system, particularly for those who do not have access to employer-sponsored or public insurance programs. As of 2023, about 9.9% of the population obtains health insurance through direct purchase, which includes plans bought in the individual market or through [[ACA Marketplace|marketplaces established by the ACA]][^4]. 

The ACA broadened access to affordable health coverage by creating subsidized health insurance exchanges where individuals without employment-sponsored insurance can access health coverage with low premiums and deductibles. These plans are affordable because of federal financial assistance programs or subsidies, primarily in the form of a premium tax credit. Marketplace insurance offers coverage options for people whose employer does not offer affordable health insurance benefits, those who are self-employed, work in the service or gig-based industries, work part-time jobs, early retirees, and low income working people[^5]. 

Enrollment through marketplace insurance reached an all-time high in 2024 when 21.4 million people selected a marketplace insurance option. Increased enrollment is likely due to increased federal financial assistance from the 2021 American Rescue Plan and the 2025 Inflation Reduction Act, boosts in funding to navigation organizations that offer outreach and enrollment assistance resources, and extended open enrollment periods[^6]. The following figure displays the increase in marketplace enrollment since the ACA went into full effect in 2014. 

![[Screenshot 2025-07-13 at 1.16.40 PM.png|700]]

Despite the improvements spurred by the marketplaces established by the ACA, privately purchased insurance often comes with higher out-of-pocket costs compared to employer-sponsored plans, which can pose a financial burden on individuals and families[^7]. The landscape of private insurance is diverse, with various plans offering different levels of coverage and cost-sharing arrangements, reflecting the complex nature of the U.S. healthcare system.

**For adults aged 40 to 65+**, ACA Marketplaces offer another avenue for private insurance purchase, with financial assistance available, but rising costs remain a concern: "the growing cost of care and the resulting increase in out-of-pocket consumer costs for those with coverage—a problem that existed before the ACA—will continue"[^8].

### Employer Sponsored Health Insurance

Employer-sponsored insurance (ESI) remains the primary source of health coverage for non-elderly residents in the United States, covering approximately 154 million people as of 2024[^9]. This form of insurance is typically provided by employers to their employees and often extends to the employees' families and, in some cases, retired employees. Employers generally bear the larger share of the insurance premium, although costs for employees has been steadily rising over the last few years. For instance, in 2022, employers paid 78% of medical premiums for single coverage plans, while employees paid the remaining 22%[^10].

Despite its widespread coverage, ESI faces ongoing challenges related to affordability and access to care, as "the exclusion of health benefits from payroll taxes has the same dollar benefit for workers at all wage levels (up to the Social Security earning limit), which results in a higher percentage exclusion (share of wages) for those with lower wages"[^11]. The cost of health benefits excluded from payroll taxes was estimated to cost the federal government $273 billion in income and payroll taxes in 2019, highlighting the significant financial implications of ESI[^11]. Additionally, the market is divided into small and large group markets based on the number of full-time equivalent employees, with different regulatory requirements for each[^11].

**For aging adults**, access to ESI decreases with age and is lower for those with lower incomes. Even with equal access, older workers may face higher premiums due to age-related health costs[^12].

Additionally, rising premiums and out-of-pocket costs can strain older workers, especially those with lower wages[^13], [[Chronic Conditions]], or mental healthcare needs as limited provider networks can affect older adults needing these services. For those looking to retire, transitioning from ESI to Medicare at 65+ is a significant change and can create significant financial and lifestyle hurdles to overcome[^14].

### Medicaid

[[Medicaid]] is a joint federal and state program that provides health insurance to people with low income or limited financial resources. Medicaid covers about 1 in 6 adults in the US and almost half of adults living in poverty[^15]. However, because income eligibility thresholds are determined by individual states, Medicaid coverage varies state-by-state, leading to significant [[3.5 Geography|geographical disparities]]. 

The main way the ACA reduced income related inequality in health insurance coverage was through Medicaid expansion[^16]. The ACA gives states the option (after the ruling of *[[NFIB v. Sebelius]]*) to expand Medicaid coverage to adults with households incomes up to 138% of the [[Federal Poverty Level (FPL)|federal poverty level]]. Currently, over **60% of non-elderly adults enrolled in Medicaid are covered through Medicaid expansion**[^17]. Before the ACA was passed, each state had its own eligibility criteria for Medicaid creating a patchwork and inconsistent network of care across the country. For example, in 2013, most states only offered full Medicaid benefits to adults with children. As a result, most non-elderly adults without dependents were ineligible for Medicaid. **ACA Medicaid expansion specifically impacts adults under 65** because Medicaid eligibility is no longer tied to whether someone has children. 

#### Expansion vs Non-Expansion States

As of 2025, 40 states and the District of Columbia have opted to expand Medicaid under the ACA[^18]. The uneven adoption across states has created a large geographical divide in access to affordable health insurance options, leaving large pockets of low-income adults ineligible for the program. About 1.4 million uninsured adults in the US fall into a "[[Coverage Gap|coverage gap]]", where one's income is too high to qualify for Medicaid but too low to qualify for subsidized marketplace insurance, particularly in states that did not expand Medicaid[^19]. 

This coverage gap exists because in the 10 states that have not adopted the ACA Medicaid expansion, the median Medicaid income eligibility limit for adults with children is 34% of the federal poverty level. All the non-expansion states except Wisconsin do not offer Medicaid coverage to adults without children[^20]. Yet, subsidies for marketplace insurance only begin at an income level of 100% of the federal poverty level because the ACA Medicaid expansion was supposed to be mandatory for all states prior to the ruling of *[[NFIB v. Sebelius]]*. So, it was assumed that adults with incomes lower than 100% of the poverty level would be covered through Medicaid, not marketplace insurance options, and they were therefore excluded[^19].

The coverage gap leads to stark geographic divides in access to affordable health insurance and health outcomes[^21]. For example, **uninsured rates in states that have not expanded Medicaid are almost double that of other states** (14.1% compared to 7.6%). Individuals in the coverage gap are disproportionately poor, working, and/or African American[^22]. Furthermore, as of 2023, nearly all the people in the coverage gap are concentrated in Southern states (97%), mainly in Texas (41.8%), Florida (19.1%), and Georgia (13.5%)[^19]. Epidemiological researchers details that,

> [!QUOTE] [Levy, Helen, and Thomas C. Buchmueller. 2025. “The Impact of Health Insurance on Mortality.” _Annual Review of Public Health_ 46 (Volume 46, 2025): 541–50. https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-publhealth-061022-042335.](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/UP2EJTMN)
> "[I]f all states had adopted the Medicaid expansion in 2014, more than 15,000 deaths would have been avoided between 2014 and 2017."

Research comparing health outcomes in expansion states vs non expansion states reveal that Medicaid expansion leads to increased use of preventative and primary care and better health outcomes. For example, patients in states that did not expand Medicaid were more likely to be diagnosed with advanced-stage rhinologic cancers (cancers of the nasal cavity or sinuses) after 2014 compared to those in expansion states[^23]. Uninsured individuals or those covered by Medicaid are also significantly more likely to receive a cancer diagnosis at later stages (III or IV) for colorectal and breast cancer in the 50-64 age group, leading to worse survival rates[^24]. Additionally, older adults lacking prescription drug coverage were less likely to adhere to their medication plan due to cost related concerns.[^25]. Therefore, **health outcomes differ across geographical lines in the US due to the unequal adoption of the ACA and Medicaid expansion.**

While the ACA expanded access to affordable health insurance across all demographic groups, the improvements were not equally distributed. Coverage increased for almost all persons living in the US, but large differences in coverage continue among Hispanic populations and immigrants compared to white people in the US, which saw the largest increase in health insurance access under the ACA[^26]. Therefore, the ACA created new forms of exclusion, segmenting access to affordable coverage based on state of residence, income, and citizenship status. 

The patchy and complex roll out of the ACA leads to stratified experiences in healthcare, even for those who are covered. Factors like immigration status, income, race, gender, disability, and state of residence determine whether you are fully covered, partly covered, or effectively excluded. The ACA and Medicaid expansion continue to be characterized by access and utilization issues such as long wait times, narrow networks of included providers, high out‑of‑pocket costs, heavy paperwork burdens, or minimal continuity of care[^27]. However, even in states that have not expanded Medicaid, the ACA has overall increased coverage for low-income populations by streamlining enrollment processes.

### Medicare

[[Medicare]] is a federal program that provides health insurance to 98.9% of US residents over the age of 65 and to people with certain disabilities younger than 65[^28]. More than 64 million US adults are enrolled in Medicare[^29]. Medicare has four different parts (A, B, C, D), each providing health insurance coverage for a different aspect of healthcare. 

Medicare Part A covers hospitalizations, hospice care, and skilled nursing facilities other than long term care. It usually does not have premiums for those who paid for Medicare through employment taxes, but does have a high deductible ($1,676 in 2025)[^30]. 

Medicare Part B covers physicians' services like preventative care, outpatient services, and certain medical equipment. Enrollees pay premiums, coinsurance, and deductibles with Medicare Part B. In 2025, the standard premium is $185.00/month and the annual deductible is $257[^30]. Medicare Part B operates on an income based premium system, where monthly premiums are based on a beneficiaries' yearly individual or joint tax return. US citizens and permanent residents already receiving Social Security or Railroad Retirement Board (RRB) when they turn 65 are automatically enrolled in Medicare Part A and B. Others may have to enroll during the Initial Enrollment Period (the 7 months around their 65th birthday) or can delay enrollment in Part B until their employer-sponsored insurance ends. People who use Medicare Part A and B are considered 'traditional' Medicare beneficiaries.

> [!QUOTE] [Ramsay, Christina, Gretchen Jacobson, Steven Findlay, and Aimee Cicchiello. 2024. _Medicare Advantage: A Policy Primer_. The Commonwealth Fund. https://doi.org/10.26099/69fq-dy83.](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/95B9K5CC)
> "People with traditional Medicare have access to any doctor or hospital that accepts Medicare, anywhere in the United States. That’s the vast majority of doctors and virtually all hospitals."

Medicare Advantage plans, or Medicare Part C, are offered through private insurers and are a combination of Part A and B. Since Medicare Advantage plans are offered through private insurance companies, they generally offer a limited network of providers for enrollees, which is a limitation of Medicare Part C. Another significant limitation of Medicare (Parts A, B, and C) for aging adults is that [[Long-term Care (LTC)|long-term care]] services at nursing facilities or at home longer than 100 days are not included, forcing aging adults to pay out of pocket, rely on Medicaid, or obtain private long-term care insurance[^31]. A benefit of Medicare Advantage plans are that they often offer coverage for vision, dental, or hearing aids for no cost or a low additional fee, which are not covered by traditional Medicare plans[^32]. The final plan, Medicare Part D, covers prescription drugs and is either included with a Medicare Advantage Plan or can be added to traditional Medicare plans.

Census data in 2022 reveals that about 40% of adults over 65 continue to carry private insurance while also covered by Medicare[^28]. Adults may be covered by both Medicare and private insurance to help with costs (such as the 20% coinsurance with Medicare part B with no out-of-pocket max), to expand their limited network with a Medicare Advantage Plan, or to access coverage for prescription drugs. **Dual insurance highlights some key gaps in Medicare's policy because adults feel as if they cannot fully rely on Medicare to cover their health needs or that they cannot afford the associated healthcare costs with Medicare coverage alone.** 

Even with guaranteed coverage as an older adult in the US, health discrepancies still exist in who can afford, utilize, and access high quality health care[^34]. To support low-income individuals afford Medicare-related health costs, the federal government mandates that all US states offer Medicare Savings Plans through Medicaid (also called dual Medicare-Medicaid coverage), which helps reduce the financial burden of premiums, deductibles, coinsurance, and prescription drug coverage[^35]. 

Approximately 16% of all people enrolled in Medicaid are also enrolled in a Medicare Savings Plan[^29], but the income eligibility criteria, wrap around services included, and enrollment processes differ between states[^29]. The discrepancies in Medicare Savings Plans across geographical boundaries is a further example of how health and insurance coverage are dependent on state-by-state policies and [[Health Equity]] initiatives[^36].

![[Screenshot 2025-07-27 at 7.34.01 PM.png|700]]

To qualify for Medicare as a person younger than 65 with a [[3.3 Disability|disability]], you must either have received [[Social Security Disability Insurance|Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI)]] benefits for 24 months, have Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS or Lou Gehrig’s disease), or have End Stage Renal Disease (ESRD). To qualify for SSDI, applicants must have about 10 years of work history, with at least 5 years of work history in the last decade, and must wait 5 months after SSDI approval before receiving cash benefits. Additional barriers to successfully securing Medicare benefits include the complex enrollment process for SSDI, which can take individuals months or even years to complete[^37]. After receiving SSDI benefits, an individual must wait an additional 24 months before they are eligible for Medicaid coverage. 

The two-year Medicaid waiting period for adults with disabilities was intended to be a cost reducing measure and ensure benefits were supporting people with life-altering, long term disabilities that may impact one's ability to work[^38]. However, the 29-month total waiting period for SSDI and Medicare causes tremendous hardship for people with disabilities because they are more likely to be uninsured before receiving SSDI benefits, and therefore, are less likely to have access to consistent healthcare options during this waiting period[^37].

The Commonwealth Fund report that about 39% of people experience some disruptions in their health insurance coverage during the two-year waiting period[^39]. While there is limited data on how many people with disabilities are in this coverage gap and their experience waiting for Medicare eligibility, researchers estimate there are:

> [!QUOTE] [_FACT SHEET: Medicare Two-Year Waiting Period for People with Disabilities_. n.d. Medicare Rights Center. https://www.medicarerights.org/pdf/two_year_waiting_period_fact_sheet.pdf.](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/X7W37K32)
> "1.8 million people with disabilities are caught up in this waiting period for Medicare. Since SSDI recipients are unable to work, they cannot access the principal source of coverage for people under 65—employer-sponsored insurance. Instead, people in the Medicare waiting period generally obtain coverage if they qualify for Medicaid, or if they can pay the premium to continue under their former employers plan under COBRA."

 Medicare enrollment through SSDI also has an income threshold, or Substantial Gainful Activity (SGA), which stipulates that people cannot earn over a certain income limit during the waiting period, or they risk terminating their disability benefits. The SGA policy creates barriers for people with disabilities who are interested in participating in the workforce, negatively impacts their mental and physical health[^40], and reinforces harmful stereotypes that people with disabilities do not meaningfully contribute to society[^41]. For example, the Commonwealth Fund reports that:

> [!QUOTE] [Williams, Bob, Adrianne Dulio, Henry Claypool, Micheal Perry, and Barbara S. Cooper. 2004. _Waiting for Medicare: Experiences of Uninsured People with Disabilities_. No. 786. The Commonwealth Fund. https://www.commonwealthfund.org/publications/fund-reports/2004/oct/waiting-medicare-experiences-uninsured-people-disabilities.](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/ITK2D4FG)
> "Many participants suffer irrevocable physical and mental deterioration during the waiting period. Foregoing doctor's visits, treatments, medications, and rehabilitation therapy has resulted in irrevocable damage to their physical and mental health. Others talk about the stress and worry of being uninsured with a disability, and how that has affected their overall well being... Most believe that without health coverage and Medicare, they cannot return to work, regain mobility, participate in their communities, and stop depending on family members and friends for their basic needs. Rather, they live constrained lives, fearful of their next health crisis."

Overall the two-year waiting period contributes significantly to the complexity and disparities present in the US health insurance landscape, and has considerable impacts on the quality of life for US adults with disabilities[^42].

As individuals approach 65, understanding the transition to Medicare is crucial, and protections against age discrimination are provided by the [[Civil Rights Act]] and the [[Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)]]. Looking ahead, the rising cost of care and subsequent increases in out-of-pocket expenses remain a pressing concern for aging adults[^43].
