## Intro
The Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) represent the complex, non-medical factors that profoundly influence an individual's health outcomes, encompassing everything from [[Socioeconomic Status (SES)|socioeconomic status]] and education to neighborhood environment and access to healthcare. Recognizing the critical impact of these determinants, this document serves as an exploration of various strategies and interventions designed to effectively address them. 

Herein, we delve into actionable recommendations and approaches aimed at mitigating the adverse effects of SDOH, fostering healthier communities, and promoting equitable well-being for all. This resource is intended to guide efforts in developing and implementing impactful solutions that tackle the root causes of health disparities.

## Intersectionality
The interplay between [[3.5 Geography]] and health insurance is undeniable.  Geographic location acts as a key determinant of access to both insurance and healthcare services. Addressing these disparities requires a multi-faceted approach, including investments in rural infrastructure to improve [[Telehealth]] access, targeted efforts to increase insurance coverage in underserved areas, and broader systemic changes to ensure a more equitable distribution of healthcare resources across all regions of the United States.

Health instability issues are particularly amplified for low-wage workers because their jobs often offer inconsistent health coverage or inadequate health paid sick leave, which exacerbates their health challenges. Low-wage workers are also more likely to work in labor-intensive roles, may be exposed to environmental hazards more frequently, and may require more frequent health visits to manage [[Chronic Conditions]] or pain. Improving work accommodations for aging, low-wage workers could be an important intervention for reducing morbidity, functional limitations, and health inequities ([mcarthur2025WorkplaceAccommodationsLowwage](zotero://select/groups/5952825/items/VTUH97WG)).
