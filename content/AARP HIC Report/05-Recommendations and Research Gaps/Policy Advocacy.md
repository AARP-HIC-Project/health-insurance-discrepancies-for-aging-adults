### Racial and Ethnic Disparities

Minority individuals with disabilities, particularly Black and Hispanic adults, experience poorer potential access to healthcare. Miller et al. noted that these individuals are less likely to have a usual source of care and more likely to have a suboptimal usual source of care[^3]. Access to health insurance is more problematic for middle-aged adults with disabilities, with rates of being uninsured increasing[^3]. This highlights the need for targeted interventions to address racial and ethnic disparities in [[Healthcare Access]] for individuals with disabilities.

## From Socioeconomics Page

### Advocacy for Structural Barriers

The findings suggest that advocacy for policies that address structural barriers to care, such as low Medicaid reimbursement rates and lack of accessible facilities, is essential. Dong et al. recommended that AARP and other advocacy groups should push for policies that not only expand coverage but also address these structural barriers[^2]. This would help ensure that increased insurance coverage translates into improved healthcare access and utilization for individuals with disabilities.

### Targeted Interventions

Targeted interventions are needed to address the specific healthcare access barriers faced by different disability groups. For example, caps on out-of-pocket spending could benefit permanently disabled individuals, particularly those who are economically disadvantaged and have multiple chronic health conditions[^4]. Additionally, further research is needed to understand the financial burden faced by temporarily disabled adults and to ascertain whether insurance characteristics, such as high deductibles or spending caps, are linked to this burden[^4].

### Addressing Socioeconomic Disparities

Addressing socioeconomic disparities is crucial for improving healthcare access for individuals with disabilities. Policies that aim to reduce poverty and increase access to health insurance for low-income individuals with disabilities could help mitigate unmet healthcare needs[^5]. Additionally, targeted outreach and education efforts could help enhance community awareness and access to healthcare services for minority individuals with disabilities[^6].
