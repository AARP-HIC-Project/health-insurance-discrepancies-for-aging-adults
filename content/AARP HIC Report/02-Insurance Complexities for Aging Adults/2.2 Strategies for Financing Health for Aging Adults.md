# Financing Health Care for Aging Adults

For aging adults in the United States, the cost of healthcare is not merely a concern—it's a relentless financial burden that forces challenging decisions. From the high premiums of employer-sponsored plans to the daunting out-of-pocket expenses in the pre-Medicare years and the catastrophic costs of long-term care, older Americans are constantly navigating a system designed to extract significant personal wealth. This note explores the profound financial challenges aging adults face in paying for their health, detailing the difficult choices they are compelled to make and the strategies they employ to survive this high-stakes landscape

## Employer-sponsored Plans and Early Retirement

[[Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance (ESI)|Employer-sponsored insurance (ESI)]] is the core of the U.S. health coverage system, yet a closer look at the data reveals a significant affordability crisis, even for those who are "covered." 

Despite its prevalence, ESI presents significant affordability challenges. Federal law does not mandate that all employers offer health benefits, and even for those with access, the financial burden can be substantial. A notable 43% of working-age adults with employer plans report difficulty affording their healthcare [^1]. This financial strain contributes to widespread dissatisfaction, with nearly half of all insured adults expressing negative perceptions due to high premiums and steep out-of-pocket costs [^2]. Counterintuitively, individuals with ESI are more likely to report cost-related problems than [[Medicaid]] enrollees [^3], underscoring that private insurance does not inherently guarantee financial protection.

For aging adults, these systemic issues are magnified, leading to profound life consequences. The combined pressure of high ongoing costs and the potential loss of coverage effectively compels many older workers to delay retirement. This forces them to remain in the workforce longer than desired, solely to maintain access to health insurance.

The period between leaving employment and qualifying for [[Medicare]] (typically age 65) is particularly challenging. The U.S. healthcare system's strong linkage of insurance to employment [^4] creates a critical coverage gap that necessitates careful strategic planning for early retirees. Furthermore, affordability is not uniformly experienced; factors such as age, marital status, race, and employment history significantly influence an individual’s capacity to retire early with adequate health coverage [^5].

#### The High-Stakes Financial Landscape of Pre-Medicare Health

The period preceding [[Medicare]] eligibility presents a high-stakes financial landscape for aging adults, often marked by considerable anxiety regarding healthcare affordability. Over a quarter of individuals aged 50 to early 60s lack confidence in their ability to afford health insurance in the coming year, a concern that nearly doubles when considering retirement [^6]. This apprehension is well-founded: a 2023 KFF Survey revealed that nearly half of all adults (45%) worry about their capacity to cover medical bills in the event of illness or accident [^7]. Furthermore, 28% report paying more for treatment or services than initially anticipated due to complex insurance structures, including high deductibles, co-pays, and surprise billing [^7]. For aging adults navigating this pre-Medicare phase, these unpredictable expenses can quickly deplete savings and exacerbate financial insecurity, posing a direct threat to their health and well-being, particularly for those managing [[Chronic Conditions|chronic health conditions]] on fixed incomes.

This pervasive concern about healthcare costs is well-founded. Recognizing these significant financial vulnerabilities, the [[Affordable Care Act]] (ACA) was enacted to address these pervasive affordability challenges, aiming to sever the direct link between income and insurance access by providing a crucial lifeline for many in this age group, particularly through subsidized marketplace plans. Health insurance coverage improved substantially among populations with income less than 138% [[Federal Poverty Level (FPL)|FPL]], with Medicaid expansion making impressive strides in reducing health inequity and increasing access for low-income populations. Kwon et al. (2019) observed that those who gained insurance post-ACA often had racial minority status, unstable employment, low-income histories, and poor health conditions, highlighting the ACA's impact on vulnerable groups [^8]. By expanding [[Medicaid]] and creating subsidized marketplace plans, the ACA successfully reduced income-related inequality in health coverage, with the most significant gains seen among the lowest income brackets [^9].

The Medicaid expansion has been particularly beneficial to older populations. Research showed that among the populations who gained the most coverage from Medicaid expansion were well-educated and employed individuals aged 55-64, with those holding a bachelor’s degree or higher being 14.24% less likely to be uninsured compared with individuals who had less than a high school education. These findings may influence states’ decisions with respect to Medicaid expansion [^10].

However, the ACA's impact has been uneven. The Supreme Court's decision to make Medicaid expansion optional for states created a "[[Coverage Gap]]" in non-expansion states, affecting over 1.4 million uninsured Americans [^11]. For those in states that did not expand Medicaid, this [[Coverage Gap]] is especially punitive for older, low-income adults who are not yet 65 and remain ineligible for any affordable coverage. This leaves millions of the poorest working-age adults in a cruel paradox: too poor to qualify for subsidized marketplace plans, but not eligible for Medicaid in their state. As Rosenbaum and Wilensky state, "[t]o provide coverage for the very poorest Americans who remain ineligible for Medicaid but too poor for subsidized private health insurance... Closing the coverage gap created by [[NFIB v. Sebelius]] represents the ACA’s most pressing piece of unfinished business" [^12].

Even for older adults currently able to afford health insurance, their long-term financial stability faces significant threats due to the evolving landscape of healthcare financing, including potential changes to Medicare, Medicaid, and Social Security. This broader uncertainty amplifies the vulnerability of this population, making the pre-Medicare period a particularly critical time for strategic financial planning to ensure sustained access to affordable healthcare.

### The Coverage Gap Between Employment and Medicare

For most non-elderly Americans, health insurance is tied to employment. Over 60% of people under age 65 have employment-sponsored health insurance [^13].

While the share of uninsured remained at about 8% in 2023, rates varied by age and poverty level. Age is also indicated as a major factor in an individual's access to health insurance. In fact, "Adults ages 19 to 64 generally have lower coverage rates than those under age 19 and adults age 65 and older. That’s because their coverage is often directly tied to employment" [^14]. 

Insurance coverage is inextricably linked to employment and yet socioeconomic barriers still create discrepancies in [[Healthcare Access]][^15]. This places low-wage workers in a particularly vulnerable position. As one review notes[^16], 

> [!QUOTE]  [mcarthurWorkplaceAccommodationsLowwage2025](zotero://select/groups/5952825/items/VTUH97WG)
> low-wage workers experience a disproportionate burden of chronic diseases due to socioeconomic disadvantages and the nature of their work environments, which often involve occupational hazards, physical demands, stress, lack of health insurance, and limited access to paid sick leave.

For aging adults, the period between leaving the workforce—whether by choice, retirement, or job loss—and becoming eligible for Medicare presents a significant health coverage gap. The high cost of [[Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance (ESI)|employer-sponsored insurance (ESI)]] often creates "[[Job Lock]]" [^17], compelling many older workers to delay retirement solely to maintain their health benefits. This is a substantial concern, as 31% of older adults with employer plans already report difficulty affording their care [^7].

For those who do transition out of employment, navigating this gap requires understanding limited, complex, and often expensive insurance options. The first pathway is the [[COBRA|Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (COBRA)]], which allows individuals to temporarily continue their existing employer plan. While COBRA offers critical continuity of care with the same doctors and networks, its prohibitive cost [^18] renders it an unsustainable long-term solution for most.

Consequently, the [[ACA Marketplace|Affordable Care Act (ACA) Marketplace]] emerges as the primary long-term alternative for many early retirees. This platform serves as a vital safety net, offering access to individual plans with income-based subsidies designed to significantly reduce premiums. The ACA has successfully reduced the number and duration of uninsured spells following job loss. However, its impact on the employer-sponsored system is complex; research indicates the ACA did little to resolve coverage inequality within ESI and may have even widened certain disparities. 

This can create an incentive for lower-wage workers to opt out of increasingly expensive employer plans in favor of more affordable public subsidies [^9], potentially leaving employer's insurance pools with a comparatively higher-earning, and possibly healthier, group of employees. As the average retirement age continues to rise and traditional employment patterns evolve, an increasing number of individuals will rely on the ACA to bridge this critical coverage gap until they reach Medicare eligibility [^19].

Understanding these options and their inherent complexities is crucial for aging adults as they plan to navigate this high-stakes pre-Medicare landscape, necessitating strategic approaches to ensure continuous and affordable health coverage.

## Option 1: "Job Lock" as a Healthcare Financing Strategy

One significant, often involuntary, strategy aging adults employ to finance their healthcare is through what is known as "[[Job Lock]]". This phenomenon arises directly from the U.S. healthcare system's strong reliance on employer-sponsored insurance (ESI). For many individuals, especially those nearing retirement age but not yet eligible for Medicare, leaving the workforce means the sudden loss of their health benefits. To avoid this critical coverage gap and maintain access to often more comprehensive and affordable insurance, individuals are compelled to remain in their jobs longer than they might otherwise desire. This includes those who wish to retire or need to stop working due to their own health or family caregiving responsibilities. In essence, staying employed becomes a primary, albeit constrained, means of financing their ongoing healthcare needs.

Evidence of this widespread practice comes from an October 2018 cross-sectional online survey study of community-dwelling U.S. adults aged 50 to 64 years. The study found that 14.1% reported keeping a job and 11.4% reported delaying or considering delaying retirement specifically to retain employer-sponsored health insurance[^20].

However, this reliance on [[Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance (ESI)|employer-sponsored insurance (ESI)]] becomes increasingly unstable with age. As individuals approach retirement, they may face age-related job discrimination, career changes, disability, or a shift to part-time work, all of which can disrupt or terminate their health coverage. 

This instability breeds significant anxiety. A 2020 study found that more than 18% of individuals aged 50-64 had avoided seeking care or not filled a prescription due to cost in the past year. The same study also noted that more than a quarter of people in their 50s and early 60s lack confidence in affording health insurance in the next year, a concern that nearly doubles when looking ahead to retirement[^6]. This fear is not unfounded, as the transition from ESI to Medicare can be fraught with complexity, often requiring individuals to switch long-standing providers and navigate a new, confusing system[^21].

## Option 2: Navigating COBRA and the ACA Marketplace

### COBRA Affordability

The Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act ([[COBRA]]) allows individuals to temporarily continue their same employer-sponsored plan after leaving a job. While this maintains crucial network and PCP continuity, the cost is often prohibitive. According to KFF's 2023 Employer Health Benefits survey, the average annual premium for an employer-sponsored family plan almost reached $24,000[^22]. The individual becomes responsible for paying the full premium—plus a 2% administrative fee—making it an unsustainable option for most retirees.

### ACA & Marketplace Affordability Challenges

While the [[Affordable Care Act|ACA]] [[ACA Marketplace|marketplace]] serves as a crucial alternative for those without employer-sponsored insurance, its affordability for early retirees presents significant challenges. A considerable number of individuals find their income, often from retirement account withdrawals, is too high to qualify for premium subsidies. Even for those who do qualify, the underlying costs can remain substantial; for instance, the average deductible for a Silver-level Marketplace plan in 2024 exceeded $5,000 for an individual [^23].

Furthermore, an offer of "affordable" [[COBRA]] or coverage through a spouse's employer plan can disqualify individuals from receiving ACA subsidies altogether, forcing them to bear the full, often burdensome, cost of coverage. While [[Health Savings Account]] (HSAs) can help manage high deductibles common in Marketplace plans, these funds cannot be used to pay the insurance premiums themselves, adding another layer of financial complexity [^24] [^25].

For example, the average cost for a Silver Plan under the ACA for two non-smoking adults aged 58, without subsidies, could be as high as $1,797 per month [^25]. The specific costs are highly variable by state, county, and subsidy eligibility, making it difficult for older adults to determine their projected expenses. Many turn to tools like [KFF's Health Insurance Marketplace Calculator](https://www.kff.org/interactive/subsidy-calculator/) to estimate their particular situations [^26].

### ACA has limited options and is expensive

Building on the challenges of affordability, the [[Affordable Care Act|ACA]] Marketplace, while a crucial avenue for health coverage, often presents aging adults with a limited array of truly viable options, especially when cost is a primary concern. Even though ACA subsidies are designed to reduce premiums, a significant number of early retirees find their income levels—often from retirement account withdrawals—either limit their eligibility or exclude them entirely from these vital financial supports. This leaves them to bear the full, often substantial, cost of premiums, which can be a significant and unexpected burden during a period of reduced income [^4].

Consequently, the practical choices available within the Marketplace become constrained. While tools like Health Savings Accounts ([[Health Savings Account|HSAs]]) can help manage high deductibles and other qualified medical expenses, HSA funds cannot be used to pay for the insurance premiums themselves. This further highlights that for many aging adults, navigating the ACA Marketplace means confronting not just high costs, but also a restricted selection of plans that fit their financial realities.

## Option 3: Dealing with the Realities of the Coverage Gap

For millions of Americans, the period between leaving the workforce and becoming eligible for Medicare at age 65 is not merely a transition, but a perilous [[Coverage Gap|coverage gap]]. Despite the existence of options like [[COBRA]] and the [[Affordable Care Act|ACA]] Marketplace, their prohibitive costs or limited suitability often leave aging adults with no truly affordable or comprehensive health insurance alternatives. This forces many into a state of being uninsured or underinsured, precisely when stable [[Healthcare Access]] becomes most critical, exposing them to profound health and financial risks.

### The Risks of the Gap: Forgoing Care and Losing Continuity

The consequences of falling into this gap are potentially severe. Lacking adequate insurance forces individuals into an impossible dilemma: either forgo necessary medical care and risk severe health complications, or seek treatment and face potentially devastating financial repercussions. Research indicates that over 18% of people in their 50s and early 60s reported forgoing needed medical care or prescriptions due to cost[^20]. This can turn manageable chronic conditions into acute medical crises, leading to worse health outcomes and higher costs in the long run.

Beyond the immediate financial burden, losing employer-sponsored insurance often means severing a long-standing relationship with a primary care physician (PCP) or difficulties in establishing care with a new PCP"[^27]. For older adults, this disruption is more than an inconvenience; their health outcomes are closely tied to the continuity of care that a trusted PCP provides. This relationship is essential for managing multiple conditions, tracking medication changes, and ensuring effective preventive screenings. Research published in the Annual Review of Public Health Journal of the Association of American Medical Colleges has shown that strong continuity of care is linked to better health outcomes, including improved management of conditions like diabetes and hypertension and higher rates of life-saving preventive screenings[^28].

Maintaining this consistent relationship with a PCP is a widespread challenge in the U.S., exacerbated for aging adults by two key barriers:
*   **Limited Provider Networks:** Many older adults struggle to find in-network PCPs, a difficulty that is often more pronounced for individuals enrolled in Medicare and Medicaid plans [^29]
*   **Employer-Based Insurance:** The nation's predominantly employer-based insurance system means that a change in employment—whether through retirement, layoff, or a new job—can put at risk long-standing patient-doctor relationship.

The difficulty of finding new in-network PCPs, particularly on some public or Marketplace plans, leaves patients disconnected from the system when they need it most. Faced with these daunting realities, older adults are compelled to employ specific and often drastic strategies to secure affordable health coverage during this vulnerable period.

## Funding Strategies 

Faced with this landscape, older adults must employ specific and often drastic strategies to secure affordable health coverage. Below are five strategies that many older adults commonly employ in order to keep from falling into the [[Coverage Gap]].

#### Strategy 1: Proactive Income Management to Maximize ACA Subsidies

For early retirees utilizing the ACA Marketplace, proactive income management is a critical strategy to maximize premium subsidies and manage pre-Medicare healthcare costs. Subsidy eligibility is based on Modified Adjusted Gross Income ([[Modified Adjusted Gross Income|MAGI]]) a specific calculation of a household's taxable income. By actively managing their MAGI, individuals can directly influence the amount of financial assistance they receive, gaining substantial control over their healthcare expenses rather than being dictated by overall wealth[^30].

Navigating insurance options can be particularly challenging for those planning early retirement, leading many to explore ACA marketplace plans. These plans often involve the use of Health Savings Accounts ([[Health Savings Account|HSAs]]) to help cover high deductibles and other qualified expenses. However, it's important to note that HSAs cannot be used for premium payments [^25], and the significant out-of-pocket costs during this period, including deductibles and drug costs, can rapidly deplete savings.

The goal is to keep annual MAGI within the most advantageous range for subsidies (typically between 138% and 400% of the Federal Poverty Level). Common methods include:

1.  **Controlling Withdrawals:** Carefully planning withdrawals from tax-deferred retirement accounts like traditional 401(k)s or IRAs, as these distributions count as income.
2.  **Utilizing Different Savings Buckets:** Relying on post-tax savings (like a Roth IRA or standard savings accounts) for living expenses in certain years, as these withdrawals do not count toward MAGI.
3.  **Timing Roth Conversions:** Strategically converting funds from a traditional IRA to a Roth IRA to "realize" income in a controlled way, either before retirement or in a low-income year.

By managing their income streams, many retirees can dramatically reduce their monthly premiums.

> [!QUOTE] [published:HealthInsuranceMarketplace2024](zotero://select/groups/5952825/items/IH95NUW6)
> _For example, a 62-year-old couple with a MAGI of $78,000 might qualify for a subsidy worth over $1,200 per month, while the same couple with a MAGI of $90,000 could see that subsidy shrink dramatically, costing them thousands more per year._

#### Strategy 2: Leveraging Medicaid as a Critical Safety Net

While proactive income management is a strategy primarily for middle-income retirees, [[Medicaid]] serves as the fundamental financing strategy for those with very low incomes. The Patient Protection and Affordable Care Act ([[Affordable Care Act|ACA]]) provided states with the option to expand Medicaid eligibility to individuals whose income falls below a specific poverty threshold[^4].

For pre-[[Medicare]] adults (aged 19-64) who have lost a job or have minimal income, finding coverage through Medicaid expansion is often the only path to comprehensive, affordable healthcare [^11]. The impact of this state-level policy is profound and varies significantly by [[3.5 Geography|state]]. In states that have expanded Medicaid, eligible adults gain access to care that substantially reduces their risk of accumulating medical debt and improves their overall financial stability.

In stark contrast, non-expansion states, such as Texas, contain a large [[Coverage Gap|coverage gap]]. In these states, many low-income older adults earn too much to qualify for traditional Medicaid or subsidies, yet too little to afford plans on the ACA Marketplace without assistance. For this vulnerable population, the presence or absence of Medicaid expansion is the single most important factor determining whether they have access to affordable healthcare[^31].

#### Strategy 3: Exploring Community-Based and Alternative Models

Beyond traditional federal and employer-sponsored programs, some aging adults explore alternative and community-based models to finance their healthcare, often out of necessity when other options are insufficient or unaffordable. These can include relying on **spousal coverage** or maintaining **part-time work** to retain some form of employer-sponsored benefits. However, these are often limited solutions, as spousal plans may not cover all needs, and part-time work may not offer comprehensive benefits or be sustainable for health reasons.

For others, non-traditional approaches emerge.[[Health Care Sharing Ministries|(HCSM)]], for example, are faith-based organizations where members contribute monthly payments to share the medical expenses of other members. While these ministries offer a financing strategy for some, they are not insurance and are not required to comply with Affordable Care Act ([[Affordable Care Act|ACA]]) regulations, such as covering pre-existing conditions or providing essential health benefits. This path requires caution due to the significant limitations and risks, as payments for care are not guaranteed [^32].

Another alternative is  ([[Short-Term Limited Duration Insurance|Short-Term Limited Duration Insurance (STLDI)]]). These plans often appeal to individuals seeking lower premiums, though this comes with the trade-off of potentially less comprehensive coverage and higher out-of-pocket costs. For certain consumers, particularly those ineligible for ACA subsidies or with specific, temporary coverage needs, STLDI can offer broader choices and more affordable premiums compared to ACA plans, which are sometimes criticized for their "one-size-fits-all" approach and narrower provider networks. For instance, a 64-year-old in Phoenix, AZ, might find unsubsidized ACA bronze plans costing $680, while STLDI plans could range from $161.57 to $1,702.78, demonstrating a potential for significant savings. STLDI can also serve as a "lifeline" for those who have missed ACA enrollment periods[^33].

However, the limitations of [[Short-Term Limited Duration Insurance|STLDI]] are significant, especially for older adults. The average age of an STLDI enrollee is 34, indicating it is not typically a long-term solution for this demographic. These plans frequently do not cover essential medical expenses such as preventive care, substance abuse treatment, or mental health services. Furthermore, even government departments acknowledge that STLDI enrollees face an "increased risk of higher out-of-pocket expenses and medical debt, reduced access to health care, and potentially worse health outcomes."

> [!QUOTE] [BidenShortTermHealth2024](zotero://select/groups/5952825/items/96AMCQHP)
> *Their objection to STLDI is not that it is low-quality but that it is of sufficiently high quality that millions of consumers are choosing it as a reasonable alternative to Obamacare. STLDI is too good, so the Departments are trying to make it bad. It is too comprehensive, so the Departments want to make it less comprehensive. The NPRM reveals the Departments’ actual purpose is to boost Obamacare enrollment by punishing consumers who make what the Departments—not Congress—believe to be the “wrong” choice.*

As of 2025, the Biden administration's rule changes have severely limited the duration and renewability of [[Short-Term Limited Duration Insurance|STLDI]]STLDI plans at the federal level. While they remain an option for temporary coverage, they are no longer a realistic long-term alternative to comprehensive health insurance. Consumers must be acutely aware of these limitations and the new disclosure requirements when considering STLDI[^33].

#### Strategy 4: Medical Debt

Medical debt imposes a profound financial burden, frequently forcing individuals to deplete their life savings, incur substantial credit card debt, or even declare bankruptcy [^1]. "Nearly one in four older U.S. adults spent at least $2,000 out of pocket on health care last year" [^34], highlighting the significant financial strain even for those with some coverage.

This burden is not equally distributed. Systemic inequities mean that Black and Hispanic older adults [^35], who often have substantially lower incomes and accumulated savings compared to their white counterparts [^36], are disproportionately vulnerable to financial fallout from medical emergencies when uninsured or underinsured. Such financial strain exacerbates existing health disparities, contributing to poorer health outcomes and, in some cases, potentially increasing mortality rates, as limited access to care due to cost can delay or prevent necessary treatment. These disparities underscore the critical role of [[Social Determinants of Health]] in influencing health outcomes.

The problem extends beyond large, catastrophic medical events. Many adults, including those with insurance, remain vulnerable to unaffordable out-of-pocket costs. "About four in ten adults (41%) report having debt due to medical or dental bills," and approximately half of all adults would be unable to pay an unexpected medical bill of just $500 in full without resorting to debt [^2]. *(See chart above)* Aging adults often use debt services—like credit cards, personal loans, or payment plans—to manage mounting healthcare expenses. While offering temporary relief, these options carry high interest rates, trapping individuals in a debt cycle that worsens financial strain and can delay or prevent necessary future care. This financial insecurity is particularly acute for those on fixed incomes, facing increasing health needs due to [[Chronic Conditions|chronic conditions]], or reduced cognitive functioning due to degenerative diseases or other factors [^37].

#### Strategy 5: Planning for Long-Term Care Costs

Beyond immediate health insurance needs, a critical and often overlooked aspect of financial planning for aging adults is the immense and often unpredictable cost of [[Long-term Care (LTC)]]. The need for LTC is a source of profound anxiety for older adults, who are keenly aware that the existing insurance system is ill-equipped to handle it. This gap between the predictable need for care and the lack of an affordable payment system represents one of the most significant unresolved challenges facing aging Americans.

This financial threat is substantial. There's a widespread "misperception that Medicare covers long-term care expenses," as Grace Smith notes, when in reality, traditional Medicare primarily covers short-term, skilled nursing care for rehabilitation, not the ongoing custodial care that most people eventually need[^38]. This critical gap leaves many vulnerable to exorbitant costs. For instance, the annual median cost for long-term care services in the United States in 2024 ranged from $24,700 to $116,800, depending on the type of service[^39]:

*   The annual cost for a semi-private room in a nursing home was $111,325, an increase of 7% from the previous year.
*   The annual cost for a private room in a nursing home was $127,750, an increase of 9% from the previous year.
*   The annual median cost for an assisted living community was $70,800, an increase of 10% from the previous year.
*   The annual cost for homemaker services was $68,640, while the cost for home health aide services was roughly $75,500.

These costs can vary greatly depending on the state and the specific services required. A KFF survey found that "almost six in ten (57%) adults 65 and older are at least 'somewhat anxious' about affording the cost of a nursing home or assisted living facility if they needed it"*(See Table Below)*.

![[4large-shares-of-adults-ages-50-64-and-those-with-lower-incomes-are-anxious-about-affording-long-term-care.png|600]]

This situation is a "huge issue that's going to drive more people to try to qualify for [[Medicaid]]" as many are "not retiring with enough money to pay for long-term care"[^38]. This creates a "middle-class squeeze," where individuals who "worked hard all their life... are falling through the cracks" because they earn too much for Medicaid but cannot afford private [[Long-term Care (LTC)|LTC]] insurance or the substantial out-of-pocket costs[^38].

For those with limited assets, Medicaid becomes the primary payer for long-term care, but only after individuals undergo a "spend-down" process, depleting their assets to very low levels, often impoverishing themselves and their families[^38]. As one Reddit user describes, "The way it generally works in the US is that, when the time comes, you move into a nursing home and then pay them until you're out of money. When that happens, you stay where you are and Medicaid pays for your care until you pass away"[^4]. This reality underscores the significant financial risks and anxieties associated with aging, with another user warning of "a tsunami coming when the boomers get elderly and dementia, and the adult kids and spouses will get crushed"[^4].

While private long-term care insurance exists as an option, it is not a widespread solution, as "only a small percentage of the population has private LTC insurance, leaving most people vulnerable to the high costs of care"[^38]. Long-term care insurance is also a significant cost *(See Table Below)*, especially for those with lower socioeconomic status. Additionally, not everyone is eligible for the insurance, as "long-term care insurance company may reject coverage if you have pre-existing conditions, in poor health, over 70 or already in long-term care" [^40]. Furthermore, many insurance companies do not offer LTC coverage; the American Association for Long-term Care Insurance (AALTCI) reports that only six insurance companies offer these policies: Bankers Life, Mutual of Omaha, National Guardian Life, New York Life, Northwestern Mutual and Thrivent.

## Conclusion

The financial realities of healthcare for aging adults in the U.S. are stark: pervasive costs lead to widespread anxiety, medical debt, and often, the difficult choice between essential care and financial stability. Strategies like "job lock," navigating complex marketplaces, or even relying on community models are often born out of necessity, not preference, as individuals strive to bridge critical coverage gaps. Ultimately, the ability of older Americans to access and afford the care they need hinges on more than personal planning; it demands systemic solutions that alleviate the crushing financial pressures and ensure health security is not a luxury, but a fundamental right.
