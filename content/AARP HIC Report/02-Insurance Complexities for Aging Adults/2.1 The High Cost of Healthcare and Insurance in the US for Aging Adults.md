# The High Cost of Healthcare and Insurance in the US

In the United States, the cost of healthcare is not just high; it's a formidable barrier that disproportionately burdens aging adults,. This note unpacks the core reasons behind these inflated expenses—from exorbitant service prices and complex insurance structures to the unique challenges faced by older populations—and reveals how these costs translate into difficult choices about essential medical care, insurance coverage, and even retirement itself.

## US Healthcare is Expensive for Aging Adults

The U.S. spends significantly more on healthcare per capita than other country[^1]. Despite higher spending, the U.S. does not achieve better health outcomes[^2]. This contrast is particularly evident when looking at outcomes for aging adults. The reasons for this inflated medical spending are many, such as the high cost of services such as hospital stays, physician and clinical services, and prescription drugs[^3]. And these costs have risen steadily making future affordability of health insurance a critical issue for aging adults. As the cost of medical care, prescription drugs, and hospital services increases, insurers can pass these costs on to consumers through higher premiums to cover the expenses of providing healthcare services to their members [^4]. 

### Providers Charge Higher than Reimbursement Rates

Many providers charge more than what [[Medicare]] or [[Medicaid]] will reimburse, which limits care options for those relying on these programs. The impact is even more significant for individuals who are [[00-Inbox/Uninsured]] or [[Underinsured]], often leaving them to face high out-of-pocket costs or forgo care altogether. Adding to the complexity of provider charges, the involvement of private insurers introduces another layer of cost and negotiation that often disadvantages aging adult consumers.

### Private Insurers Make it More Expensive

"Public-payer programs (particularly Medicare and Medicaid) tend to pay hospitals rates that are lower than the cost of delivering care... while private payers historically have paid about twice as much as public payers"[^3]. Private insurance providers then often negotiate rates with healthcare providers—rates that may not reflect the actual cost of services. 

For aging adults enrolled in private insurance plans, this can translate into higher premiums and increased out-of-pocket expenses. Additionally, the reliance on employer-sponsored health coverage ([[Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance (ESI)|ESI]]) can leave older adults vulnerable to shifting coverage options and rising costs, particularly as they transition out of the workforce. 

The cumulative effect of these high costs, provider practices, and private insurance dynamics directly translates into significant affordability challenges for many Americans, particularly as they navigate their later years.

### Affordability

While having health insurance coverage reduces mortality and morbidity for people living in the US, it does not uniformly resolve all [[Healthcare Access]] issues because the types of barriers faced by adults vary by their coverage. Cost is a significant barrier that people with and without insurance face when seeking medical care. A substantial number of individuals in their 50s and early 60s lack confidence in their ability to afford health care, "when they look ahead to retirement"[^5]. In 2020, it was reported that "Nearly half of adults in their 50s and early 60s aren’t sure they’ll be able to afford health insurance after they retire, and two-thirds worry about the potential effects of health policy changes"[^5]. One third of adults have forgone or delayed seeking medical care due to affordability concerns, 1 in 5 US adults have not filled prescription medications due to the price, and 41% of US adults owe a [[Medical Debt]][^6].

### The Affordability Crisis: When an Insurance Card Isn't Enough

Despite the [[Affordable Care Act|ACA]] expansion, research also finds that simply having insurance does not guarantee access to affordable care. Socioeconomic and health factors significantly influence unmet healthcare needs. 

The high cost of premiums, deductibles, and copayments remains a formidable barrier for many, regardless of their insurance type. A 2023 Commonwealth Fund survey found that "large shares of working-age adults say they struggle to afford health care, including 43 percent of those with employer coverage, 57 percent with marketplace or individual plans, and 45 percent with Medicaid"[^7].

This struggle with affordability forces individuals to make impossible choices. In 2022, 28% of adults reported delaying or forgoing medical care, prescription drugs, or mental health care due to cost *(See Chart Below)*. This behavior has direct consequences for health. According to a KFF survey, "Almost 1 in 6 (15%) insured adults with insurance problems also said those problems caused a decline in their health"[^8].

Financial barriers directly contrast with perceptions of insurance coverage rates, as a large majority of adults (91%) actually have health insurance and self-report good health (85%), yet many still face barriers to accessing medical care[^9].

[[Medicaid]] expansion has been instrumental in increasing insurance coverage for working-age adults with disabilities. Strong evidence suggested that Medicaid expansion led to increased Medicaid coverage among working-age adults with disabilities in expansion states (3.2% to 5.0%)[^10]. A study by Xiaobei Dong et al. found that Medicaid expansion led to a significant increase in Medicaid coverage among this population, ranging from 3.2% to 5.0% in expansion states[^11]. However, the study also noted that this **increased coverage did not translate into improved healthcare access or service use outcomes.** and no statistically significant effects were found on health care access or service use outcomes (e.g., having a usual source of care, unmet medical needs, ER visits). The lack of significant impact on access and utilization suggests that simply expanding coverage may not be enough to address the healthcare needs of this population.

## Barriers to Healthcare Access: Financial and Non-Financial

### Financial Barriers to Care

Cost remains a primary concern for many, particularly for people enrolled in high deductible plans, especially those with [[Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance (ESI)|employer-sponsored insurance (ESI)]][^13]. Even modest increases in healthcare costs can significantly reduce medication adherence, underscoring how many older adults are living on the financial margins. Nearly one in four older Americans spent at least $2,000 out-of-pocket on healthcare last year[^14]. At the same time, 45% of adults report being worried about their ability to pay medical bills if they become seriously ill or injured[^15]. This financial strain often forces difficult decisions—such as skipping doses, delaying refills, or forgoing treatment altogether—which can lead to worsened health outcomes over time.

The rate of healthcare costs is indeed escalating at a rapid pace, placing increasing financial pressure on aging adults. "Older adults face high out-of-pocket healthcare costs, averaging "$6,668 in 2020, an increase of 38% since 2010 ($4,843)"[^16]. This significant rise means that those on fixed incomes or with seemingly adequate insurance are increasingly burdened, often leading to difficult choices between essential medical care and other living expenses.

### Non-Financial Barriers to Care

Beyond direct financial concerns, various non-cost-related barriers significantly impede healthcare access. These include difficulty finding a provider that takes one's insurance, long wait times for appointments, challenges with transportation to health clinics, work restrictions (such as minimal or no paid sick leave), and issues finding childcare. These barriers often reflect differences in the characteristics of people enrolled in various types of coverage, as well as inherent limitations within the plans themselves.

Adults enrolled in Medicaid, for instance, are more likely to face non-cost access barriers than those with other forms of coverage. Data from the 2022 National Health Interview Survey reveals that 24% of adults enrolled in Medicaid reported facing at least one of the following: difficulty finding appointments or locating a doctor that takes their insurance, lengthy travel to the health clinic, or transportation issues during business hours [^9]. Comparatively, 18% of those with non-group plans (e.g., marketplace or individually purchased insurance) and 15% of people with an employer-sponsored insurance plan reported at least one non-cost related barrier [^9]. Issues like finding timely health appointments or a provider who takes public insurance may reflect limited provider networks available to many Medicaid enrollees. Overall, about 1 in 5 adults across all coverage types report a barrier to accessing healthcare unrelated to cost, particularly when seeking [[Mental Health Services]] [^15].

Furthermore, certain demographic groups may face unique or more frequent non-financial barriers. [[Qualitative Research]] with African Americans in the U.S. South reveals that key barriers to healthcare access for Black U.S. residents include scheduling issues, appointment availability, lack of transparency in insurance coverage, and a low number of primary care clinics in rural and low-income areas[^17]. Older communities that suffer from more significant intersections with other [[Social Determinants of Health|social determinants of health (SDOH)]] also face these issues, such as how certain types of disabilities (cognitive impairments, hearing deficits, and lower body functional difficulties) are particularly associated with experiencing access barriers[^18].


## Conclusion

The U.S. healthcare system's exorbitant costs, driven by factors like high provider charges and the dynamics of private insurers, create an unsustainable financial burden for aging adults. Even for those with insurance, pervasive high premiums, deductibles, and out-of-pocket expenses, compounded by non-financial hurdles such as limited provider networks, long wait times, and transportation issues, lead to a profound affordability and access crisis. This forces many older Americans to make agonizing choices: delaying or forgoing essential medical care, accumulating significant medical debt, or struggling to manage chronic conditions and disabilities under immense financial strain. This escalating crisis underscores that the high cost of healthcare is not merely an economic challenge, but a direct threat to the health, dignity, and security of our aging population, demanding urgent and systemic solutions.
