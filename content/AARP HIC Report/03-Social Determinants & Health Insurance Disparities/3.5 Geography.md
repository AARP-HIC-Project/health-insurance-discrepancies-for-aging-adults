# The Geography of Disparity: How Location and Insurance Shape Health Outcomes for Aging Adults

Geography, encompassing everything from state lines to the rural-urban divide, stands as a critical social determinant of health in the United States. Where a person lives can profoundly influence their access to health insurance, the quality of care they receive, and ultimately, their health outcomes. 

The overarching context of the U.S. healthcare system suffers from unequal distribution of resources, opportunities, and outcomes across regions and geographies. This relationship is particularly stark for aging adults who make up a majority of rural populations and are often more vulnerable to the limitations of healthcare access due to their remote locations, mobility issues, and higher prevalence of [[Chronic Conditions|chronic conditions]]. The cumulative effects of these geographic disparities can be detrimental to the health and well-being of this population, exacerbating existing health inequities as they age. 

### State Policy and the Health Insurance "Coverage Gap"

The passage of the [[Affordable Care Act|Affordable Care Act (ACA)]] aimed to reduce the number of [[Uninsured|uninsured]] Americans, but [[NFIB v. Sebelius]] made the law's [[Medicaid]] expansion optional for states[^1]. This decision created a fractured landscape of healthcare access, where an individual's eligibility for affordable insurance is dictated by their state of residence. In states that have not expanded Medicaid, a [[Coverage Gap|coverage gap]] has emerged[^2][^3], leaving millions of low-income adults without a viable path to insurance. This gap disproportionately affects specific regions and populations, particularly in rural areas and the South[^4]. 

An analysis from the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities notes that the millions caught in this gap are a varied group of "essential workers, parents caring for children, older and younger adults, and diverse in terms of race and ethnicity—but they all have something in common. They live in states that have failed to adopt the Affordable Care Act's (ACA) Medicaid expansion"[^5]. The consequences of this policy-driven disparity are severe. Residents in these areas often face significant barriers in accessing healthcare, including limited provider networks, lengthy wait times, and high out-of-pocket costs for services[^6]. As a result, they are more likely to go without necessary care, delaying treatment for conditions until they become severe or requiring expensive emergency interventions. This not only leads to poorer health outcomes but also perpetuates a vicious cycle of delayed care, increased healthcare expenditures, and further exacerbates existing health disparities in these communities. The effects are particularly pronounced among aging adults who require regular check-ups, chronic disease management, and [[Preventive Care|preventative care]] to maintain their health and mobility.

### The Rural-Urban Divide: A Widening Gap in Health

Beyond state policy, the physical geography of rural versus urban living presents another layer of disparity. These factors contribute to significantly worse health outcomes and underscores a broader problem: rural communities often struggle to provide consistent and accessible preventive healthcare services, leading to potentially poorer health outcomes for people living in these areas.

The health challenges in rural America are systemic and deeply rooted in [[Socioeconomic Factors|socioeconomic factors]]. Compared to their urban counterparts, "Rural residents tend to have lower incomes, a lower education level, are less insured, poorer housing options, and limited access to supermarkets and healthy food options"[^7][^8].

Other statistics paint a grim picture of this divide. According to recent research, rural adults see a "20% higher all-cause mortality rate, life expectancy more than 3 years below their urban counterparts, 11% higher rate of hypertension, 40% higher rate of diabetes, 40% higher rate of heart disease, 30% higher rate of stroke, and a 100% higher rate of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. They also experience higher rates of depression, suicide, and drug-related deaths"[^7][^9]. While the ACA did lead to coverage gains in rural areas, residents continue to face higher [[Uninsured|uninsurance]] rates and greater financial burdens from healthcare costs, often due to lack of options for plans or providers[^10].

### Compounded Disparities for Aging Adults

For aging adults, these geographic disparities are not abstract statistics but lived realities with cumulative consequences. The older adult population is growing more rapidly in rural areas than in urban ones, placing a particularly vulnerable demographic in the very locations with the fewest resources[^11][^12]. 

> [!QUOTE] [Bureau, US Census. n.d. “In Some States, More Than Half of Older Residents Live In Rural Areas.” Census.Gov. Accessed July 24, 2025. [https://www.census.gov/library/stories/2019/10/older-population-in-rural-america.html](https://www.census.gov/library/stories/2019/10/older-population-in-rural-america.html).](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/W962ATUL)
>
> "The percentage of older adults is higher in rural areas than in the rest of the United States, with 17.5% of the rural population being 65 years or older compared to 13.8% in urban areas."

As people age, the lifelong effects of inconsistent insurance coverage, limited access to care, and poorer baseline health become more pronounced. For example, "rural areas also experience a higher incidence of cancer... which may be due, in part, to reduced access to preventive and screening services in rural areas" and "Healthcare facilities in rural areas are more likely to be understaffed than comparable facilities in urban areas"[^13]. Most concerning is that rural healthcare facilities in the United States are indeed closing at an alarming rate as well, with more than 100 rural hospitals have closed in the past decade--exacerbating already dire shortages of medical professionals and services in these regions. This trend of hospital closures has significant consequences for the quality and availability of healthcare services in rural areas, making it even more challenging for residents to access necessary care when they need it most:

> [!QUOTE] [Cohen, Steven A., and Mary L. Greaney. 2023. “Aging in Rural Communities.” _Current Epidemiology Reports_ 10 (1): 1–16. [https://doi.org/10.1007/s40471-022-00313-9](https://doi.org/10.1007/s40471-022-00313-9).](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/2NM8U54E)
> 
> "The National Institute of Minority Health and Health Disparities considers rural populations a health disparity group, in part because these populations have higher rates of mental health concerns, chronic diseases, and worse general health outcomes than non-rural populations"

The intersection of age, location, and [[Socioeconomic Status (SES)|socioeconomic status]] creates unique and formidable barriers. For older adults with histories of homelessness, the Medicaid coverage gap in non-expansion states can be a final, insurmountable hurdle to obtaining care[^14]. For those with [[3.3 Disability|disabilities]], the lack of insurance can be catastrophic. A 2011 study by Iezzoni and colleagues found that "36.0% of uninsured persons with disabilities reported being unable to get necessary medical care, compared with 9.5% of uninsured, nondisabled persons"[^15]. These challenges are exacerbated in rural areas where healthcare providers are scarce and transportation is a significant barrier.

## Conclusion

Geography is not a passive backdrop to health but an active force that shapes it. Through the mechanism of state-level policy decisions, particularly the refusal to expand Medicaid, an individual's zip code can determine their access to affordable health insurance. This political geography is overlaid with the physical geography of the rural-urban divide, which concentrates socioeconomic disadvantage and poor health outcomes in specific regions.

For aging adults, these factors create a landscape of compounded risk. The health disparities that accumulate over a lifetime are magnified by the systemic barriers tied to their location. Addressing these inequities requires a multi-pronged approach. Closing the Medicaid coverage gap is a critical first step, but it must be paired with targeted investments in rural healthcare infrastructure, [[Telehealth|telehealth]] expansion, and policies that address the underlying social determinants of health. Without such a "matrix of solutions," [^16], an individual's place of residence will continue to be one of the most powerful and inequitable predictors of their health and longevity in their later years.
