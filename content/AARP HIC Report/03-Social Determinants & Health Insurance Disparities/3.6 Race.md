# Race as a Fundamental Barrier to Health Equity for Aging Adults

Health insurance coverage is a critical gateway to healthcare services, yet racial and ethnic minorities, including aging adults, face persistent barriers to obtaining and maintaining adequate coverage. Race-based discrepancies serve as a profound [[Social Determinants of Health|social determinant of health]], deeply influencing insurance coverage and health outcomes for aging adults in the United States. Significant racial and ethnic disparities exist in areas like [[Healthcare Access|healthcare access]], utilization, cost, and care among older adults, that persist even for those with near-universal Medicare coverage[^1].  Addressing these disparities is becoming increasingly urgent, especially given that [[People of Color|people of color]] are projected to make up nearly half of the population aged 65 and older by 2060[^1]. 

When comparing data by race and/or ethnic groups, it is important to consider that race is a social construct, not a biological factor, and is often self reported during hospital admissions or survey participation. Examining race as a demographic factor in the [[Uninsured|uninsured]] rates of US adults helps to highlight instances where systemic or structural racism influences people's access to health insurance, and further research on the root causes of racial disparities is needed to address equity issues in health insurance coverage[^2].

## Racial Disparities Among Uninsured People in the US

[[3.6 Race|Racial inequities]] continue to shape who lacks health insurance among U.S. adults. In 2022, [[People of Color|people of color]] made up more than 62% of the non-elderly uninsured population, despite representing about 46% of the non-elderly population in the US overall[^3]. Hispanic populations, Native Americans, and Alaska Natives have the highest uninsured rates of all racial and ethnic groups in the US where 19.1% of Native Americans and Alaska Natives are uninsured and 18% of Hispanic persons are uninsured. Additionally, a considerable portion of Native Hawaiian and Pacific Islanders (12.7%) and Black people (10%) in the US are also uninsured. The uninsured rate of the white population in the US is disproportionately smaller than other racial and ethnic groups at 6.6%[^4]. 

For aging adults belonging to a minority group specifically, the landscape of insurance coverage remains similarly uneven. In 2019, for adults aged 60-64 years, significant uninsurance rates persisted: 6.9% for Black adults, 15.1% for Hispanic adults, and 7.0% for White adults across all income levels[^5]. And this [[Medicaid coverage gap]] is a stark illustration of the disproportionate struggle for people of color to obtain and maintain health insurance coverage, particularly in the years leading up to Medicare eligibility[^6].:

For example, data from 2022 show a pronounced stratification in health insurance coverage by citizenship status: recent immigrants (under five years in the United States) had an uninsured rate of 30.3%, rising to 33.1% among those with longer residence, while the comparable rates among U.S.-born and naturalized citizens were far lower at 7.7% and 9.5%, respectively[^3].

The convergence of racial and income disparities creates a particularly vulnerable set of circumstances for aging and older adults. Low-income minorities with poor health have significantly lower odds of being insured compared to high-income Whites with good health[^7].

Though Medicaid expansion under the [[Affordable Care Act|ACA]] helped to offset some racial disparities in the uninsured population, but states that did not expand Medicaid (primarily southern and western states) have large Communities of Color impacted by the [[Coverage Gap|Medicaid coverage gap]][^8]:

These disparities are exacerbated in states that have not expanded Medicaid under the [[Affordable Care Act|Affordable Care Act (ACA)]], as uninsured rates in non-expansion states are higher across most racial and ethnic groups, as "[e]vidence suggests coverage disparities during the pandemic widened in non-expansion states"[^5]. In non-expansion states, though 23.8% of adults with incomes less than 138% of the [[Federal Poverty Level (FPL)]] were uninsured, and the coverage disparity between low-income Hispanic adults and low-income White adults was 11.4 percentage points larger in non-expansion states versus expansion states[^5]. 

This exemplifies how while some of the racial inequalities in healthcare coverage can be explained by unequal Medicaid expansion, most of the health insurance coverage discrepancies between racial groups can be explained by issues related to ESI. The unequal insurance rates between racial groups in the US reflects disparities in job and income opportunities for People of Color because People of Color are overrepresented in jobs that do not offer health benefits like service industry jobs, part-time positions, or contract work[^12].

> 
> In 2019, 2.2 million uninsured adults (aged 19-64) with incomes below the poverty line fell into this gap because they resided in non-expansion states. A staggering 60% of this coverage gap population were people of color, with 617,000 Black adults and 613,000 Latino adults affected[^8]. While this data primarily focuses on adults up to age 64, it highlights a systemic issue that impacts individuals as they transition into older adulthood, often carrying a history of inadequate coverage and deferred care.  

This gap also draws into question whether work requirements for Medicaid, such as those included in the [[The Big Beautiful Bill|Big Beautiful Bill]], are appropriate since the majority of these working and uninsured adults are not offered insurance by their employer, are concerned about the high costs of health insurance, or are unsure of how to access Medicaid or marketplace insurance due to the complex enrollment process. 

Health analysts argue that "these requirements have been defended [by policymakers] as necessary to encourage work among the 'able-bodied' poor, reflecting assumptions that the poor must be coerced to work as a condition of insurance. These assumptions have historical roots in racist beliefs that Black people are lazy and have a poor work ethic"[^12], and such rhetoric was popularized by President Ronald Reagan during his attacks on welfare recipients[^13]. The demographics of the uninsured population being predominately low-wage working adults disrupts the racially charged sentiment that low-income people lacking insurance or covered through Medicaid are unemployed.

## The Role of Immigration Status

Furthermore, immigration status plays a significant role in widening racial disparities in health coverage. Uninsured Asian, Black, Hispanic, and Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander (NHPI) individuals under age 65 are less likely to be eligible for coverage because they include larger shares of noncitizens who are subject to eligibility restrictions for Medicaid and Marketplace coverage[^9]. Changes to immigration policy can further reduce enrollment for lawfully present immigrants and citizen children in immigrant families, perpetuating these inequities into their later years.

These restrictions can create a cycle of uninsurance and deferred care that carries into older adulthood. ## Race and the Affordability Crisis: When Coverage Fails to Guarantee Care

Even when insured, disparities persist. A study on ambulatory care utilization found that while disparities were smaller among insured individuals compared to the uninsured, significant differences remained, particularly among insured Hispanics in better health. This suggests potential issues with access to [[Preventive Care|preventative care]] for minorities, even with insurance, or an overuse of medical care by healthy white individuals[^15].

This underscores that insurance alone is not a panacea; the quality and [[Culturally Competent Care|cultural competency of care]], as well as the design of health plan benefits, are crucial for improving [[Preventive Care|preventative care]] use across all populations[^15]. If health plans do not adequately cover culturally appropriate services or if providers lack [[Cultural Competency|cultural competency]], insured individuals from minority groups may still face barriers to effective care.

Race-based discrepancies in health insurance and access are deeply intertwined with [[Socioeconomic Factors|socioeconomic factors]]. Financial strain can force families into difficult choices, impacting their long-term economic stability, as Black and Hispanic older adults typically have lower incomes and savings. 

In particular, older adults of color face significant financial barriers due to lower incomes and savings, reflecting systemic disadvantages like fewer years of education, lower lifetime earnings, disparities in job opportunities, limited access to retirement benefits, and less inherited wealth. These economic realities translate directly into problems paying for healthcare. About one in six older Black adults (16%) and one in seven older Hispanic adults (14%) report difficulties paying for healthcare, significantly higher than the 8% of older White adults who report similar issues[^1]. These financial burdens include premiums, copayments, and deductibles, which disproportionately affect older adults of color, even those with Medicare coverage[^1].

## Race, Insurance, and the Impact on Health Outcomes

 Research shows that within all races individuals face poorer [[health outcomes]] due to a lack of insurance[^17], but the disparities are more pronounced _between_ different racial groups. And for aging adults of color, the lack of adequate insurance and the presence of financial barriers directly translate into poorer health outcomes and limited access to necessary care during years when they tend to develop and suffer from higher incidence and prevalence of [[Chronic Conditions|chronic illness]] than younger individuals. Complicating matters, older Black and Hispanic adults report poorer overall health status and higher rates of chronic conditions compared to their White counterparts[^1]. 

> [!QUOTE] [Hamel, Liz, Ana Gonzalez-Barrera, Marley Presiado, Nancy Ochieng, Juliette Cubanski, and Tricia Neuman Published. 2024. “Five Facts About Older Adults’ Health Care Experiences by Race and Ethnicity.” _KFF_, April 29. [https://www.kff.org/racial-equity-and-health-policy/issue-brief/older-adults-health-care-experiences-by-race-ethnicity/](https://www.kff.org/racial-equity-and-health-policy/issue-brief/older-adults-health-care-experiences-by-race-ethnicity/).](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/RGC6H4UB)
> 
> "Black and Hispanic older adults report poorer health status and higher rates of chronic conditions such as [[Hypertension|hypertension]] compared to their White counterparts."

Lack of adequate insurance coverage is a significant factor in delaying diagnosis, leading to more advanced stages of diseases like cancer at detection for minority groups[^18][^1]. This means that by the time they receive a diagnosis, for many older adults of color, their conditions may be more severe and harder to treat, leading to worse prognoses, worse health outcomes, and higher healthcare costs in the long run. 

Minority individuals with disabilities, particularly Black and Hispanic adults, also experience poorer potential access to healthcare, including being less likely to have a usual source of care or more likely to have a suboptimal one[^19]. Black and Hispanic older adults experience higher rates of hospital admissions and are more likely to "receive care in the lowest-rated hospitals"[^1][^20][^21]. This often means they receive care in facilities with fewer resources, higher patient-to-staff ratios, and less specialized expertise.

The cumulative stress of navigating healthcare and insurance systems marked by historical discrimination and economic disadvantage can lead to what is known as [[Weathering|weathering]][^22][^23], or chronic exposure to social and economic stressors, including racism, that leads to premature aging and health deterioration among minority populations.

> 
> The constant stress of living within a racist society could lead to poor health for marginalized groups...people experiencing high levels of chronic stress as a result of their identities and circumstances have poorer health outcomes.

For older adults of color, this means that even when biological and genetic factors are held constant, they frequently encounter significant health disparities due to the lifelong impact of these stressors. This manifests as higher rates of [[Chronic Conditions|chronic conditions]] and poorer overall health status, making healthcare needs more complex and costly over time.

The cumulative effect of delayed care can lead to a lifetime of poorer health, especially in comparison to their White counterparts. These disparities are compounded by issues in healthcare access and utilization, creating a [[Syndemic|syndemic]] cycle where [[1.2 The Benefits of Having Health Insurance|lack of access contributes to worse health]], which in turn makes healthcare more complex and costly. The higher rate of chronic illness and delayed medical care due to a lack of insurance creates a challenging set of circumstances for BIPOC aging adults as they are also subject to higher prevalences of disability as a result of this syndemic. 

## Compounded Vulnerabilities: Other Intersecting Social Determinants

Access to health insurance coverage becomes complex and individual for people facing multiple barriers due to different parts of their identity. The dynamics between interconnected forms of discrimination and systems of power can be explained by [[Intersectionality|intersectionality]], a term coined by legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw that examines how overlapping social identities—such as race, gender, class, and sexuality—interact to create unique and compounded experiences of oppression or privilege[^24]. Focusing on improving access to health insurance coverage for people facing complex and intersecting barriers based on their sociocultural, economic, and citizenship status can be a way to improve health insurance coverage for all because their experience often reveals where the most pertinent issues and gaps in care lie.

#### Race and Disability 

The high prevalence of disabilities among older American Indian/Alaskan Native (AI/AN), Hispanic, and "other race/multiracial" groups[^25] underscores the compounded vulnerability faced by these populations as they age, given their already poorer access to care. Disability often increases healthcare needs and costs, making adequate insurance even more critical.

For American Indian women, including elderly tribal members, lack of insurance is a significant barrier, often leading them to forego care due to inability to afford out-of-pocket expenses, including medications[^26]. This is compounded by both challenges related to aging out of certain types of coverage and a lack of awareness about available assistance programs for aging adults--which can lead to using the emergency room as a primary source of care[^26]. This reliance on emergency services for primary care is inefficient, costly, and often results in delayed treatment for chronic conditions. ## Conclusion: Achieving Health Equity Beyond Coverage

Race-based discrepancies profoundly shape the health and insurance experiences of aging adults. These [[Health Disparities|health disparities]] are not accidental but are the result of deeply entrenched systemic inequities, economic disadvantages, and historical factors that limit access to adequate insurance and quality healthcare. 

While policy interventions like the ACA and COVID-19-era expansions can improve coverage rates across racial and ethnic groups, they do not eliminate disparities. Underlying structural factors, such as income inequality, state Medicaid expansion decisions, and immigration policies, continue to drive unequal access to health coverage. The potential rollback of recent coverage expansions could exacerbate these disparities[^27]--highlighting the fragility of progress when systemic issues remain unaddressed.

The challenges faced by aging adults of color often intersects with other social determinants of health, such as [[3.3 Disability|disability]], educational disparities (i.e., [[3.2 Education and Health Insurance Literacy|health insurance literacy]], and citizenship/immigration status, to create compounded vulnerabilities that further entrench health inequities. As the older adult population in the U.S. becomes increasingly diverse, with [[People of Color|people of color]] projected to comprise nearly half of adults aged 65 and older by 2060[^1], addressing these race-based disparities becomes an urgent imperative.
