# Defining the Knowledge Gap: What is Health Insurance Literacy?

[[AARP HIC Report/06-Glossary/Health Literacy|Health literacy]], [[Health Insurance Literacy|health insurance literacy]], and insurance coverage are interconnected factors that significantly influence health outcomes. 

Health literacy, which refers to the ability to obtain, process, and understand basic health information and services needed to make appropriate health decisions[^1] "is a critical need in the United States, with research consistently demonstrating that more than one-half of the U.S. population does not understand basic health insurance terms" and "[w]ithout adequate levels of HIL, consumers are unable to understand the financial and health implications of insurance plans, often leading to delayed care or foregoing needed care"[^2]. [[Health Insurance Literacy|Health insurance literacy]] is a new concept that addresses the extent to which consumers can make informed purchase and use decisions. It is defined as the "knowledge, ability, and confidence to find and evaluate information about health plans, select the best plan for his or her family for their own or their family's financial and health circumstances, and use the plan once enrolled" [^3].  Higher levels of health literacy and health insurance literacy are generally is associated with better healthcare utilization, including increased use of preventive services, more appropriate utilization of emergency care, and improved adherence to medical treatments, as well as an "increased utilization of preventive health care services"[^4]:

![[Screenshot 2025-07-23 at 9.15.03 PM.png|100%]]

Health insurance literacy is particularly crucial for older adults, who often face complex healthcare systems and may require greater assistance in understanding their coverage and benefits. Furthermore, it can positively impact their ability to navigate medical terminology, financial aspects of healthcare, and potential risks associated with different treatment options. Conversely, lower levels of [[AARP HIC Report/06-Glossary/Health Literacy|health literacy]] can lead to increased risk of medical errors, delayed diagnoses, and poorer health outcomes, particularly among vulnerable populations. These individuals may struggle to understand their treatment plans, leading to suboptimal care, as understanding health plans and their associated costs is fundamental to making informed decisions about healthcare.

## System Complexity and Health Literacy: Difficulties for Older Adults
The increasing complexity of the US insurance landscape and ongoing healthcare policy changes create confusion and disparities as adults seek care covered by their insurance and attempt to enroll in Marketplace or Medicaid coverage. Health systems researchers argue that "the U.S.’s pluralistic, patchworked system of health insurance creates unstable coverage, which has consequences for the health care and health status of the population"[^5]. 

The [[Affordable Care Act]] has made understanding health insurance even more crucial for both insured and [[Uninsured|uninsured]] Americans, as it has changed plans and introduced new marketplaces[^6]. Consumers currently exhibit limited health insurance literacy, as "[r]esearch suggests that health insurance is a complex financial product for consumers"[^3] which causes individuals to often make less-than-optimal choices due to factors like low literacy, lack of clear information, or information overload and make poor decisions. This can be seen in mental models of decision-making[^1]:

Even among individuals with higher education levels, significant disparities in [[Health Insurance Literacy|health insurance literacy]] persist and are compounded by ongoing healthcare policy changes and the increasing complexity of insurance products. The complexity of health insurance is a significant barrier for consumers[^7], particularly the elderly (who tend to have more complicated medical conditions and can suffer from higher rates of degenerative cognitive conditions) and can widen existing disparities in health access and outcomes. There is a need for developing financial health literacy (FHL) assessment tools and interventions, especially for vulnerable groups like aging adults as, "[p]eople with advanced age, cognitive impairment, low education/income, females and racial and ethnic minorities are at risk for low FHL, making them important targets of future interventions"[^8].

> [!QUOTE] [Kim, Jinhee, Bonnie Braun, and Andrew D. Williams. 2013. “Understanding Health Insurance Literacy: A Literature Review.” _Family and Consumer Sciences Research Journal_ 42 (1): 3–13. [https://doi.org/10.1111/fcsr.12034](https://doi.org/10.1111/fcsr.12034).](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/VXZ8W9GN)
> 
> "Consumers think choosing a health insurance plan is important but they might make less than optimal health insurance choices for reasons such as low health insurance literacy, lack of information or misinformation, information overload, and time constraints."

For example, when a group of researchers set out to measure Connecticut's ACA qualified health plan enrollees' health insurance literacy in 2019. They found that, confusion even existed around basic terminology like "premium" and "deductible." Many incorrectly defined "premium" as "the best type of health insurance you can buy" or "a bonus you get at the end of the year if you stay covered." And calculating out-of-pocket costs by combining deductible and co-pay information was particularly challenging. Only 31% of respondents correctly calculated their out-of-pocket expenses in a hypothetical scenario[^9] where they asked, "Suppose that under your health insurance policy, hospital expenses are subject to a $1000 deductible and a $250 per day co-pay. You get sick and are hospitalized for 4 days, and the bill comes to $6000. How much of that hospital bill will you have to pay yourself?"[^10].

Since 2019, the law has constantly been subject to regulatory changes, judicial interpretations, and legislative adjustments. Currently, there are ongoing legislative efforts that propose further changes to the ACA, such as the [[The Big Beautiful Bill|2025 Reconciliation Bill]], which includes complex provisions related to eligibility verification, repayment of excess premium tax credits, and eligibility for subsidized [[ACA Marketplace]] coverage[^11]. 

These proposed changes could potentially impact the already complex health insurance landscape, further exacerbating challenges faced by aging consumers in understanding their coverage and benefits. The combination of low health literacy and low coverage leads to delayed care and emergency costs, which lead to poor health outcomes and a [[Syndemic|syndemic]] where the cost-of-care can mutually exacerbate the timeliness-of-care and the resulting negative health outcomes for older adults. This holds especially true with current definitions and frameworks of digital literacy, skills, and competence, as they often set expectations that are too high for older adults who have low HIL and are now dealing with the proliferation of technological advancements and disruptions that are being adopted by medical companies and organizations.

### [[The Digital Divide]] as a Modern Barrier
Given the increasing digitalization of everyday life and the growing older adult population, the need for digital literacy among older adults is even more crucial, as since the [[Covid-19 pandemic]], [[The Digital Divide]] (i.e., the gap between those who have access to and the skills to use digital technologies and those who do not) has further accelerated for aging adults[^12].

> [!QUOTE]+ [Vercruyssen, Anina, Werner Schirmer, Nelly Geerts, and Dimitri Mortelmans. 2023. “How ‘Basic’ Is Basic Digital Literacy for Older Adults? Insights from Digital Skills Instructors.” _Frontiers in Education_ 8 (September). [https://doi.org/10.3389/feduc.2023.1231701](https://doi.org/10.3389/feduc.2023.1231701).](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/6T687HTB)
> 
> "Having the necessary digital literacy can increase independence, promote the physical and mental well-being of older adults, prevent cognitive decline, and is helpful to maintain identity."

These foundational skills require comprehension, memory, and dexterity, which can be affected by physical aging. Older adults lacking basic digital skills may face significant barriers to accessing online resources, managing their health insurance, and engaging with [[Telehealth|telehealth]] services. Vercruyssen and colleagues found that "what is considered as entry-level, basic digital skills, or foundational skills, can be very demanding for older adults with limited or no prior knowledge of and practice with digital technologies" and provided testimony from digital skills instructors and their clients that show how difficult basic tasks can be for some aging adults:

> [!QUOTE] [Vercruyssen, Anina, Werner Schirmer, Nelly Geerts, and Dimitri Mortelmans. 2023. “How ‘Basic’ Is Basic Digital Literacy for Older Adults? Insights from Digital Skills Instructors.” _Frontiers in Education_ 8 (September). [https://doi.org/10.3389/feduc.2023.1231701](https://doi.org/10.3389/feduc.2023.1231701).](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/6T687HTB)
> 
> "Those folders and the explorer, that is a big problem. Again and again. Recently, someone had 4,000 e-mails in his inbox and three thousand something files on his PC, and he had problems finding files... (Walter, 70)"

Currently, there is a critical gap in understanding the specific challenges faced by older adults with limited or no prior digital experience. Unfortunately, the importance of health insurance literacy has been under-studied in general. In fact, though it may seem apparent that many individuals lack clear understanding of their health insurance, it is a "widely perceived but poorly documented problem"[^7] even though, "[f]ield experiments on simplifying either the information gathering or decision making process have documented positive impacts on outcomes in a variety of health and non-health domains"[^7] like senior citizens’ Medicare Part D plan choices[^13]. 

These positive results give hope for a future where [[The Digital Divide]] is closed because these skills can be taught and the complexities can be broken down. One study found that an IRS initiative to inform people about HealthCare.gov led to increased coverage and reduced mortality among middle-aged adults[^14].

## The Tangible Consequences: The Human Cost of Low Health Insurance Literacy

### HIL's Intersections with Race
Health insurance literacy is a significant barrier to accessing and utilizing healthcare, particularly for racial and ethnic minorities and those with limited English proficiency. These disparities are amplified when combined with other SDOH like race.

> [!QUOTE] [Victor G. Villagra, M. D., M. A. Bhumika Bhuva, PhD Emil Coman, M. B. A. Denise O. Smith, and PhD Judith Fifield. 2019. _Health Insurance Literacy: Disparities by Race, Ethnicity, and Language Preference_. March 2019, vol. 25 (March). [https://www.ajmc.com/view/health-insurance-literacy-disparities-by-race-ethnicity-and-language-preference](https://www.ajmc.com/view/health-insurance-literacy-disparities-by-race-ethnicity-and-language-preference).](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/848T786F)
> 
> While these baseline survey results overall are not much different from the rest of the country, the survey uncovered a terrible inequity that puts people of color at a great disadvantage.

Minority groups face compounding difficulties even when we look across education levels. Within each group of educated individuals, studies found that racial disparities still existed as, "The uninsured rate for non-Hispanic American Indian and Alaska Native adults with an undergraduate college degree  (12.5%) was more than three times higher than that of non-Hispanic White adults (3.5%).  Regardless of educational attainment, some groups had high uninsured rates.  For example, those identifying as American Indian and Alaska Native, non-Hispanic or Hispanic, had the highest  uninsured rates at all education levels."[^15].

These implications have been persistently supported in other research, as a 2019 study conducted in Connecticut revealed that overall health insurance literacy was low, with the group of participants answering only 62% of questions correctly. Significant disparities were noted based on race, ethnicity, and language preference. The rates of correct answers were as follows: Black (53%), Hispanic (50%), White (74%), and Spanish-speaking (45%). Again, these disparities persisted even after controlling for covariates, including education levels[^16]. This research indicates that differences in insurance coverage among racial groups are directly connected to existing [[3.6 Race|racial disparities]] and indicate that the system is also designed without non-English speakers in mind.

These findings are concerning for an aging population that will be significantly more diverse in the coming decades. The increasing complexity of the healthcare system and the increased digitization of everyday life will create bigger hurdles for already disparaged minority groups. The increasing diversity of the older adult population will only exacerbate existing disparities, making it essential to address health insurance literacy as a critical component of healthcare access and equity.

### HIL and Medical Debt
Higher health insurance literacy is associated with better health outcomes including a lower risk of [[Medical Debt|medical debt]], particularly for Americans approaching retirement age[^17]. Wiltshire et al. conducted a study that "examined the relationships among health insurance literacy, health insurance factors, and difficulty paying medical bills (i.e., medical debt) in Americans age 50 to 64 years using data from the Health Reform Monitoring Survey"[^17]. 

The study found that "people with higher health insurance literacy reported lower medical debt. Type of insurance coverage did not influence medical debt. Those with annual deductibles and out-of-pocket health care costs were more likely to report having medical debt." Additionally, higher HIL was associated with a lower risk of having medical debt when accounting for income and education. The [[Intersectionality|intersectionality]] of these and other factors make navigating the health system a major challenge for many aging adults as age-related issues like cognitive decline and increased need for complex medical procedures compound the difficulties, making it even more crucial to address health insurance literacy as a critical component of healthcare access and equity.

## Conclusion

For many seniors, deciphering intricate plan details can be overwhelming, making it difficult to choose the best option or even understand existing benefits. Simplifying insurance information and processes isn't just helpful; it's a proven method to empower older adults. Clearer information leads to better decision-making, increased use of necessary healthcare services, and higher enrollment in beneficial programs. This simplification is especially critical in reducing medical debt, which disproportionately affects older adults who often struggle the most with healthcare complexity.

Furthermore, the way healthcare organizations communicate with and support their older patients is a vital factor in addressing health disparities. When organizations effectively convey information and offer robust support, they can bridge the gap between understanding insurance and achieving positive health outcomes. This ensures that aging adults fully grasp their insurance benefits and know how to access the care they need. Addressing these interconnected issues of plan complexity, affordability, and communication is essential to truly improve health outcomes and achieve health equity for our aging population.
