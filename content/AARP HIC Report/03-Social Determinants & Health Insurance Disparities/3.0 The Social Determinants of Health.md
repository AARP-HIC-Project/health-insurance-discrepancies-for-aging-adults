# The Social Determinants of Health

The health and well-being of aging adults in the United States are profoundly influenced by a complex interplay of social determinants of health (SDOH), which are the non-medical conditions in which people live, work, and age. While health insurance coverage is a critical component of healthcare access, its effectiveness is often undermined by underlying SDOH such as [[3.1 Socioeconomics]], [[3.6 Race]], [[3.4 Gender and Sexuality]], [[3.3 Disability]], and [[3.5 Geography]]. These factors, alongside varying levels of [[3.2 Education and Health Insurance Literacy]], create significant disparities in access to affordable care, leading to substantial out-of-pocket costs, delayed treatments, and ultimately, poorer health outcomes for many older Americans, even those with insurance.

This chapter describes these major SDOH and how they impact health insurance, coverage and outcomes. Their presence often results in delayed or forgone treatments, and ultimately, poorer health outcomes for many older Americans, even those who possess seemingly comprehensive insurance coverage. The current research indicates that the mere presence of insurance alone does not guarantee equitable access or optimal health when these deeper societal barriers persist. Any and all efforts to increase health equity within the aging population through insurance interventions will require a broader approach that acknowledges and addresses these SDOH, rather than solely focusing on expanding insurance coverage or improving plan design.

## What Are the Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)?

The [[Social Determinants of Health|social determinants of health (SDOH)]] refer to the non-medical factors that influence an individual's or population's health outcomes. Historically, [[Healthcare|healthcare]] treatments and assessments have focused on "downstream" effects of individual behaviors (e.g., disease/morbidity/mortality rates, delayed or avoided medical care, healthcare and insurance costs) instead of "upstream" social determinants of health that directly contribute to the circumstances in which these effects arise and give rise to disparities (e.g., [[Socioeconomic Status (SES)|socioeconomic status]], education level, employment opportunities, housing conditions, access to healthcare, and social support networks)[^1].

The field of public health has used the concept of "social determinants of health" (SDOH) to illuminate aspects of the illness experience that are not strictly biological in nature. The SDOH describe, "The conditions in which people are born, grow, live, work and age," and the influence these conditions have on health outcomes[^2]. These factors include, but are not limited to, socioeconomic status, education level, employment opportunities, housing conditions, access to healthcare, and social support networks. Many health conditions are impacted by numerous SDOH and their impact is often also dependent on the conditions of an individual's particular life circumstances. These disparities are not merely a reflection of individual choices but are rooted in systemic inequities, historical injustices, and ongoing structural racism that permeate the healthcare system and broader society. They are not merely a result of individual medical conditions or lifestyle choices, but are profoundly shaped by the broader societal context in which individuals live. For example, even with insurance, socioeconomic determinants can create significant barriers to healthcare utilization. This leads to a [[Syndemic|syndemic]] cycle of economic hardship and underuse of healthcare, with families often acting as a safety net, further straining their resources. This study highlights the limitations of focusing solely on health insurance coverage as a measure of healthcare access. It emphasizes the importance of considering the broader economic and social context in which healthcare decisions are made.

Even as individuals age and become eligible for programs like Medicare, the cumulative effects of these lifelong disparities continue to manifest, leading to significant differences in health status, access to care, and financial security. The SDOH framework works to change the discourse concerning health outcomes from that of individual responsibility to re-locating it as also directly intertwined with social structures. This reorientation promotes health transformation at the social scale, instead of only attending to it through the actions of individuals[^3]. The SDOH contribute to a significant portion of an individual's overall health by shaping their environment and directly impacting their access to care.[^4]. 

## Insurance and SDOH for Aging Adults

Beyond broad [[Socioeconomic Factors|socioeconomic factors]], age itself profoundly interacts with health insurance to shape health outcomes, particularly for aging adults. Aging adults are particularly vulnerable because as individuals transition through middle age into their senior years, they face unique vulnerabilities where the cumulative effects of disparities in insurance access and quality of care become more pronounced[^5]. 

The percentage of insurance coverage is an example of a downstream effect that has been used to understand health within the US. Currently, approximately 92% of people in the United States were covered by health insurance, but when we look at equally insured adults, we see that significant disparities exist between various groups when we look across different demographics (e.g., age, race, gender, etc). This suggests that other factors are at hand that create these disparities (i.e., social determinants), despite coverage being similar across groups. 

Even with near-universal Medicare coverage for those over 65, SDOH challenges persist that are particular to aging, including high out-of-pocket costs and barriers stemming from ageism that influence treatment decisions[^6][^7]. For instance, older adults often face substantial out-of-pocket medical costs, averaging nearly $7,000 in 2020, and complications when transitioning to Medicare, which can delay care and worsen health problems[^8]. These disparities highlight that health outcomes are deeply intertwined with sociocultural environments, explaining why certain populations experience disproportionate declines in physical functioning and access to quality care as they age despite having similar rates of coverage--suggesting that having insurance coverage alone is insufficient to eliminate healthcare inequities.

There is an urgent need for targeted interventions and policies to address these disparities as the older adult population becomes more diverse, as almost half of adults reported reduced healthcare access in June 2020, with only 38.7% attributing it to the pandemic. This improved by May 2021, but even while policies implemented during the [[Covid-19 pandemic]] led to coverage gains, significant disparities persist based on social determinants like [[Race]]/ethnicity, Medicaid Expansion Status (i.e., [[3.5 Geography|geography]]), and immigration status[^9][^10].
