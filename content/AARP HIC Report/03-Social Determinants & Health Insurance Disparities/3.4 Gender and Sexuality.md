# Gender as a Critical Determinant of Health Insurance Access

Gender and insurance status significantly influence health outcomes, impacting older adults in various ways. Gender disparities are often compounded by other SDOH such as [[Socioeconomic Status (SES)|socioeconomic status]], housing stability, disability, and sexual identity, creating complex barriers to equitable [[Healthcare Access|healthcare access]]. Overall, the research reveals a complex landscape of insurance disparities for aging adults that manifest through their gender identity, highlighting the urgent need for more equitable and comprehensive health insurance policies that address the diverse needs of all aging populations. 

### The Compounded Burdens for [[Cisgender]] Women

**Financial Difficulties Causing Delayed Care and Diagnoses**

Gendered differences in U.S. health insurance coverage, while improved by the Affordable Care Act (ACA), but persist in complex ways, impacting both access and financial burden.

Coverage gaps have narrowed and women generally have slightly lower [[Uninsured|uninsured]] rates than men, primarily due to higher Medicaid enrollment among women, particularly at younger ages. In 2023, 10% of women aged 19-64 were uninsured (9.3 million women) compared to 13% of men in the same age group[^1]. 

In 2023, 60% of women ages 19-64 received health coverage from employer-sponsored insurance, while 9% of women ages 19 to 64 purchased insurance in the non-group market and Medicaid covered 19% of women: 

![[health-insurance-coverage-of-women-ages-19-64-2023-pie-401696588.png|100%]]

Beyond direct coverage, other economic factors contribute to long-term disparities. Affordability remains a major concern, with many insured women struggling to meet deductibles. Women tend to have higher out-of-pocket expenses and use more healthcare services, indicating a greater need for comprehensive coverage, as "[w]omen are more likely to depend on their spouses for health insurance and spend a higher share of their income on health care than men, supporting the notion that gender inequalities in pay and employment increase barriers to care for women"[^2].

These financial burdens, compounded by existing wage gaps, can disproportionately impact women's financial and health stability, particularly for women of color and those with lower incomes. Being female, living at or near the poverty level, and lacking health insurance increased the odds of unmet health care needs[^3], as women consistently pay more out-of-pocket for healthcare than men, even when excluding maternity-related expenses. In 2021, women (ages 19-64) paid 20% more than men in out-of-pocket expenditures for all medical claims[^4]. Working women pay an average of $266 (18%) more out-of-pocket for healthcare per year than men, excluding pregnancy-related expenses[^5]. This totals an estimated $15.4 billion more paid out-of-pocket by working women annually[^4]:

![[US187389_Figure3.png|100%]]

These delays can be particularly devastating for aging women, as women are also diagnosed an average of 4.5 years later than men for many diseases. For example, research shows that cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death for women, yet [[Preventive Care|preventative care]] focuses on reproductive health and cancer and middle-aged women with heart disease symptoms are twice as likely as men to be misdiagnosed. Additionally, 29% of women avoid or delay [[Mental Health Services|mental health services]] due to cost, which can exacerbate conditions like anxiety and depression, which are more common in women[^4].

Older women, particularly those facing homelessness or living with disabilities, are more likely to be uninsured or reliant on public insurance[^6]. This vulnerability underscores the compounded challenges faced by older women who are already marginalized by their housing status. This reliance on public insurance, while crucial, can come with its own set of limitations regarding coverage scope and provider networks, suggesting that women may face a greater cumulative burden of health issues that necessitate robust insurance coverage as they age. These disparities are made even more worrisome when the World Economic Forum reports that efforts to close the gender gap will take another 131 years to reach parity[^5].

**The Economic Penalty of Employer-Sponsored Insurance Preventing Affordable Retirement**

Historically, women faced significant disadvantages in the individual health insurance market. Before the ACA, "women's gender was, in effect, a preexisting condition that signaled the potential for higher health care use and higher costs"[^7]. Insurers could charge women higher premiums for the same coverage, a practice known as "[[Gender Rating|gender rating]]," and could even deny coverage based on pre-existing conditions like pregnancy[^8]. 

The [[Affordable Care Act]] (ACA) banned gender rating in the individual market, but this did not significantly impact the employer-provided market, except for very small firms. Even within firms, employee contributions to insurance rates do not vary by gender, but wage offsets account for women's higher insurance risks overall[^9]. 

The gender [[Wage Gap|wage gap]] also plays a role in health insurance disparities. Cowan and Schwab showed that during prime working years, women have higher expected healthcare expenses than men[^9]. However, [[Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance (ESI)|employer-sponsored health insurance]] rates are not gender-rated in the employer-sponsored health insurance (ESI) market. This results in women experiencing lower wages in equilibrium from employers who offer health insurance. The study suggests that this accounts for roughly 10% of the overall gender wage gap, highlighting the economic implications of health insurance disparities[^9][^3].

These cost differentials point to the broader issue of the female [[Wage Gap|wage gap]]. The findings suggest a connection: "wage offsets account for women’s higher insurance risks overall"[^9]. The estimates point to a significant annual impact, with "the hourly loss in wages is roughly $1–$1.50" and a resulting "annual (extra) pay gap of $1000–$3000" [^9]. This range aligns with other estimates of healthcare cost differences between men and women. Employer-provided insurance, while seemingly equitable in terms of premiums, can indirectly transmit cost burdens onto employers, potentially influencing workforce composition and contributing to wage disparities. The observed “wage offsets” directly suggest that female healthcare costs play a role in the broader female wage gap.

For aging women, this wage disparity accumulates over a lifetime and can significantly impact a woman's financial capacity to afford comprehensive health insurance or out-of-pocket medical expenses in their later years, potentially forcing greater reliance on less comprehensive public options, on spouses for insurance, or leading to periods of being uninsured. It can also contribute to the amount of capital they have in retirement and impact their quality of life due to the same affordability concerns mentioned previously.

**Critical Gaps in Reproductive and Postpartum Care**

Before the ACA, many individual health plans offered no routine maternity benefits at all[^8]. The current federal policy mandates only 60 days of postpartum Medicaid coverage, risking loss of healthcare during a critical period. If mothers are required to re-qualify for Medicaid shortly after giving birth, they could lose healthcare when they are at more risk of dying or getting sick[^10]. Seo and colleagues reported that 35.6% of postpartum women in poverty lacked Medicaid or government medical assistance[^11].
 
Postpartum insurance disruptions are frequent among people with Medicaid coverage at childbirth, particularly among [[People of Color|BIPOC]] individuals, with 17.0% of rural BIPOC residents with Medicaid at birth becoming uninsured postpartum compared to only 3.4% of urban white residents[^12]. Black, Hispanic, and Native American individuals who are disproportionately covered by Medicaid are therefore at the most significant risk of pregnancy-related death[^13][^14].  Poor maternal and infant health outcomes can signal broader public health challenges that might eventually affect all age groups, including the elderly, in terms of resource allocation and overall community well-being[^5].

While the core issue is reproductive and postpartum health for younger women, the ripple effects through family structures and the broader healthcare system mean that elderly and aging females are not entirely immune to the consequences of these critical gaps in care. The impact is primarily indirect, manifesting as increased caregiving responsibilities, financial strain, and concerns for the health of future generations.
## Systemic Barriers for Transgender and gender-diverse (TGD) Adults

The intersection of gender, race, and sexuality further complicates access to health insurance. [[Transgender and gender-diverse (TGD)]] adults, especially including those in older age brackets, face unique barriers in health insurance coverage and access to care. 

Bolibol et al. (2023) examined the impact of the ACA and the 2015 Supreme Court ruling on marriage equality on health insurance coverage and access to care for LGBT adults. They found that while disparities in insurance coverage began to decline in 2014 with the implementation of the ACA, significant disparities in access to care persisted[^15].

In terms of insurance coverage, Hispanic straight cisgender men are identified as the group least likely to have insurance. Conversely, White transgender adults appear to have a higher likelihood of being insured compared to many other groups. Transgender adults who are "ethnoracially marginalized" are more prone to having public insurance, while straight Black women face a high likelihood of their insurance denying coverage [^16]. Generally, lesbian, gay, and bisexual adults are less likely to be insured when compared to straight adults, and sexual minority adults are also less likely to possess employer-based or private insurance[^17].

Additionally, [[LGBTQ+]] adults are more likely to rely on Medicaid as their primary source of health insurance than non-LGBTQ+ adults and will be disproportionately impacted by any future spending cuts, as they will primarily impact LGBTQ+ adults who are living with low incomes, disabled, raising children, people of color, transgender adults, and cisgender lesbian and bisexual women[^18][^18].

Furthermore, LGBT adults were significantly less likely to have insurance and more likely to report difficulty obtaining necessary medical care compared to non-LGBT adults, even though disparities in insurance coverage began to decline with the implementation of the ACA[^15]. For example, TGD people had greater odds of being uninsured compared with [[Cisgender|cisgender]] women[^19]. By 2017-19, coverage rates for LGBT adults were comparable to non-LGBT adults, but significant disparities in access to care persisted[^15]. Even among respondents who were likely Medicaid-eligible, TGD respondents had lower odds of having public insurance and higher odds of being uninsured compared with cisgender women[^19], suggesting that systemic hurdles exist in even accessing adequate publicly funded insurance for this population.

**The Battle for Gender-Affirming Care**

Transgender and non-binary patients with Medicaid coverage are more likely to face denials for hormone therapy and encounter a scarcity of in-network surgery providers[^17]. This is particularly concerning given that trans adults are generally more likely to have public insurance than private insurance, and public insurance often does not fully cover all necessary procedures and treatments and may offer less comprehensive coverage for gender-affirming care[^17].

The fragmented nature of insurance coverage for gender-affirming surgeries further exacerbates these challenges. Significant variation exists in insurance coverage for gender-affirming surgeries across different companies, states, and procedures[^20]. For instance, while most insurance companies (98%) covered chest masculinization (mastectomy), far fewer covered nipple-areola complex (NAC) reconstruction (20%), and chest feminization (breast augmentation) was covered by only 29% [^20]. This inconsistency creates confusion and acts as a major barrier to accessing needed care--**impacting TGD individuals throughout their lives, including as they age and may require ongoing or new gender-affirming treatments.**

Research indicates that increased health insurance coverage could significantly improve health outcomes for transgender individuals, underscoring the critical need for better and more consistent coverage[^21]. The barriers TGD adults face in accessing healthcare are persistent and impact them throughout their lives. For instance, the higher likelihood of facing insurance denials for hormone therapy or a lack of in-network surgery providers, especially on public insurance like Medicaid, remains a critical issue for older TGD adults who may require ongoing gender-affirming care[^17].

Overall, TGD and gender-diverse adults, regardless of age, face significant hurdles in obtaining and utilizing insurance, especially for gender-affirming care, and are more likely to be uninsured or [[Underinsured|underinsured]].

# Conclusion: Moving Beyond a Monolithic View of Gender in Healthcare

Gender, sexuality, and insurance status are critical determinants of health outcomes, with marginalized groups facing even more significant barriers to accessing adequate healthcare. The Affordable Care Act has made strides in reducing disparities, but persistent challenges remain, particularly for transgender individuals and those at the intersection of multiple marginalized identities. 

Effective policy and solutions must address the distinct challenges faced by different groups—including the economic penalties and care gaps affecting cisgender women and the fundamental barriers to coverage and affirming care for TGD individuals—to achieve true [[Health Equity|health equity]].
