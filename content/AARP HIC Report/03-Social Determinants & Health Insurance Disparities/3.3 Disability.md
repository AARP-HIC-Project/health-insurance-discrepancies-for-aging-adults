# Disability: The Convergence of Aging and Development of Disease

The prevalence of disability significantly increases with age, particularly as individuals move through their 40s, 50s, and into their 60s and beyond. For instance, while younger working-age adults (18-64) have an overall disability rate of about 11%, nearly half of all working-age adults with a disability are 50 years old or older[^1]. 

This trend culminates in a substantial proportion of older adults experiencing disability, "In 2022, 33% of adults 65 and older reported having a disability. Specifically, 6% were blind or had serious difficulty seeing even when wearing glasses, 14% were deaf or had serious difficulty hearing, 13% had difficulty doing errands (e.g., visiting a doctor’s office, shopping) alone, 21% had serious difficulty walking or climbing stairs, 8% had serious difficulty concentrating, remembering, or making decisions, and 7% had difficulty dressing or bathing"[^2]. Mobility disability, defined as serious difficulty walking or climbing stairs, is particularly common among middle-aged (45-64) and older adults (65+), affecting almost 1 in 5 and 1 in 4 individuals, respectively[^3]. Cognitive and independent living difficulties also become more prevalent with advancing age[^4].

## Coverage Disparities for People With Disabilities

Despite gains in coverage, disparities persist between between working-age adults with and without disabilities. These disparities manifest across critical dimensions, including equitable access to care, patterns of healthcare utilization, and the financial burden of healthcare costs[^6]. For example, middle-aged and older adults with hearing loss face higher costs but often do not receive the necessary services, indicating a critical gap in care and vision disability was associated with lower rates of health insurance coverage and having a usual healthcare provider, particularly among  middle-aged adults[^7]. 

> [!QUOTE] [Miller, Nancy A., Kirk ,Adele, Kaiser ,Michael J., and Lukas and Glos. 2014. “Disparities in Access to Health Care Among Middle-Aged and Older Adults with Disabilities.” _Journal of Aging & Social Policy_ 26 (4): 324–46. [https://doi.org/10.1080/08959420.2014.939851](https://doi.org/10.1080/08959420.2014.939851).](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/L6S23WPR)
> 
> In contrast to older adults, access to health insurance is more problematic for middle-aged adults and rates of being [[Uninsured|uninsured]] have increased.

Kennedy and colleagues highlighted that adults with disabilities are less likely to work, more likely to have lower incomes, and more likely to rely on public insurance compared to those without disabilities[^6]. This economic vulnerability directly translates into practical barriers where average health costs for individuals with disabilities are alarmingly 3 to 7 times higher, often due to the need for ongoing specialized care, assistive devices, and therapies for other chronic conditions[^6]. This compelling evidence unequivocally indicates that [[Socioeconomic Factors|socioeconomic factors]]—including employment status, income levels, and insurance type—exert a powerful and pervasive influence on the ability of people with disabilities to obtain necessary healthcare. 

> [!QUOTE]+ [Kennedy, Jae, Elizabeth Geneva Wood, and Lex Frieden. 2017. “Disparities in Insurance Coverage, Health Services Use, and Access Following Implementation of the Affordable Care Act: A Comparison of Disabled and Nondisabled Working-Age Adults.” _INQUIRY: The Journal of Health Care Organization, Provision, and Financing_ 54 (January): 1–10. [https://doi.org/10.1177/0046958017734031](https://doi.org/10.1177/0046958017734031).](http://zotero.org/groups/6082085/items/USLPG7QC)
> 
> Compared with working-age adults without disabilities, those with disabilities are less likely to work, more likely to earn below the federal poverty level, and more likely to use public insurance. Average health costs for this population are 3 to 7 times higher, and access problems are far more common.

Similarly, Working-age adults with disabilities are more likely to be "older, unmarried, female, and non-Hispanic, as compared to individuals without physical disabilities." Women also had significantly less formal education, lower household incomes, and were more likely to rely on public health insurance ([[Medicare]] or [[Medicaid]]) than individuals without physical disabilities"[^27] and individuals with dementia are more likely to be single or widowed and belong to racial or ethnic minority groups[^8].

Aging populations face compounded healthcare challenges due to specific demographic characteristics. Older age inherently brings a higher prevalence of co-morbid conditions, necessitating more complex and coordinated care. Being unmarried can also mean less informal support for navigating complex healthcare systems, a critical factor for for aging women, who often face similar socioeconomic disadvantages. This intersection of age, marital status, gender, and socioeconomic standing creates a complex web of barriers.

For the aging population, the numbers do not improve. Disparities exist within specific age cohorts and disability types. For instance, a study by Simpson and colleagues (2016) found that middle-aged adults (55-64 years) with a diagnosis of hearing loss incur significantly higher healthcare costs than their peers without hearing loss. Despite these higher costs, only a small fraction (12.9%) of these individuals received hearing services, pointing to a significant gap in care[^9]. 

## The Domino Effect: From Chronic Disease to Disability

As Americans age, the nature of their healthcare needs fundamentally changes. The primary health challenge is rarely a single, acute illness, but rather the compounding effect of multiple [[Chronic Conditions|chronic conditions]]. This [[Comorbidity|comorbidity]] is a defining feature of health in later life and carries profound implications for patients, families, and the U.S. healthcare system.

The escalating rates of disability in aging adults are largely driven by the accumulation and progression of chronic health conditions. Approximately 92% of older adults have at least one chronic condition, and 77% have two or more[^10] (American Psychological Association, n.d.). Conditions such as arthritis, heart disease, diabetes, and stroke are strongly linked to the development of functional limitations[^11][^12]. Studies indicate that older adults with major chronic diseases experience earlier onset and a more compressed progression of functional decline compared to their healthier peers[^12]. This highlights the critical interplay between chronic illness and the rising incidence of disability as individuals age, impacting their independence and overall quality of life.

For many older adults that have a chronic condition, their healthcare journey consists of navigating the healthcare system for treatment of acute illnesses and injuries, while _also_ managing conditions like hypertension, chronic heart disease, obesity, and COPD. Research shows that zip codes with a higher prevalence of these chronic diseases are, on the whole, significantly older.[^13] Over time, the cumulative burden of chronic conditions has a debilitating effect on the body's physical abilities.

This progression from disease to disability is a critical, and often predictable, pathway. While some events like a stroke can cause disability immediately, other conditions like diabetes and hypertension contribute to a sharp increase in physical disability as the disease progresses into its later stages[^14]. The path to disability is also inequitable. A study Chou and colleagues found that individuals with lower educational levels and females experienced higher levels of physical disability across all chronic conditions[^14].

Studies have suggested a link between comorbidities in chronic illness lead to higher rates of disability later in life. One study in Taiwan found that stroke, cancer, and diabetes lead to higher levels of physical disability at the time of diagnosis. Stroke, lung disease, and heart disease led to steady increases in disability after diagnosis, while diabetes, cancer, and hypertension led to higher increments of disability in later stages of the disease[^14]:

And older minority adults are more likely to have higher rates of Medicaid and other forms of public insurance[^16].

This shift from managing a disease to coping with a disability is the point where the need for care can fundamentally change for aging adults. It moves beyond prescriptions and doctor visits to require a web of support services, including physical and occupational therapy, dental care, and long-term care.

### Access: Unmet Needs and Negative Health Outcomes

Unmet healthcare needs due to cost were prevalent, especially among younger adults with independent living disabilities and middle-aged adults with vision disabilities[^7]. This highlights the need for targeted interventions to address insurance gaps, and access barriers, older adults with disabilities suffer from significant unmet medical, dental, and prescription medication needs--which all lead directly to poorer health.

Mahmoudi and Meade found that individuals with physical disabilities have higher odds of having unmet medical, dental, and prescription medication needs[^18]. Lisa I. Iezzoni et al. reported that 36.0% of uninsured persons with disabilities were unable to get necessary medical care, compared with 9.5% of uninsured, nondisabled persons. Similarly, 26.9% of uninsured persons with disabilities could not get necessary medications, compared with 5.3% of uninsured individuals without disabilities[^19]. These findings underscore the critical role of insurance in ensuring access to necessary healthcare services.

Finally, individuals with physical disabilities were significantly more likely to report unmet medical care, [dental care](https://www.sciencedirect.com/topics/medicine-and-dentistry/dental-procedure), or access

Working-age adults with disabilities are far more likely to rely on public insurance (Medicaid and Medicare) than private insurance[^6]. Unmet healthcare needs due to cost are a significant issue that disproportionately affects certain age groups and disability types. The Okoro (2018) study noted that these unmet needs were particularly prevalent among middle-aged adults with vision disabilities[^7]. In fact, "Individuals with physical disabilities have 75% (_p_ < 0.0001), 57% (_p_ < 0.0001), and 85% (_p_ < 0.000) higher odds of having unmet medical, dental, and prescription medication needs, respectively."[^18].

Disparities in healthcare access also vary based on the type of disability, so services need to also be tailored to change with people as they age. Many older adults accumulate conditions, but their coverage does not accumulate care options with them.

## Compounded Disadvantage: The Intersection of Age, Disability, and Race

This health burden is not distributed equally. The prevalence, diagnosis, and outcomes of these compounding conditions often fall along racial, ethnic, and socioeconomic lines, creating layers of disparity[^20]. Research shows that certain populations experience later-stage diagnoses for diseases like cancer, leading to worse outcomes.[^21] The challenges are further compounded by other [[Social Determinants of Health|social determinants of health]].

Minority individuals with disabilities, particularly Black and Hispanic adults, experience poorer potential access to healthcare[^20]. Miller and colleagues noted that these individuals are less likely to have a usual source of care and more likely to have a suboptimal usual source of care[^22]. "Among adults aged 65 years and older, half of all AI/ANs (54.9%), Hispanics (50.5%), and those who reported that they are “other non-Hispanic race or multi-racial” (49.9%) reported a disability"[^3].

## Conclusion

Disability is a social determinant that represents an escalating challenge for aging adults, intrinsically linked to the aging process and the accumulation of chronic health conditions. Despite efforts to expand insurance coverage to assist those with disabilities to access and pay for care, disparities persist in healthcare access, utilization, and financial burden for individuals with disabilities, particularly among working-age adults and minority populations. These inequities are deeply rooted in [[Socioeconomic Factors|socioeconomic factors]] and intertwined with other SDOH, leading to alarmingly high rates of unmet medical, dental, and prescription medication needs. 

The compounded disadvantage experienced by older adults, especially those from racial and ethnic minority groups, further exacerbates these issues, revealing systemic biases within the healthcare system. Ultimately, disability functions as a critical social determinant of health, directly shaping an individual's insurance coverage, access to necessary services, and overall health outcomes, underscoring the urgent need for comprehensive and equitable interventions.
