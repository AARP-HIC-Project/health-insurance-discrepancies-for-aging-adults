# Navigating the Healthcare Landscape for Aging Adults (40-65+)

This report critically assesses the state of health insurance coverage (HIC) in the U.S., highlighting profound discrepancies that impact healthcare access and outcomes for adults aged 40 to 65+. 

The research within this report is not meant to be a comprehensive or systematic review. The purpose of this study was to understand some of the main factors that are at hand in the world of aging adults that impact and are impacted by their insurance coverage and any disparities that emerge from that status.

Below is a summary of some of the main findings of the report. There is a significant amount of information that was gathered for this report and we believe that it is best experienced and understood when one can see the connections between these overlapping topics and explore the interconnectivity of these themes.

# Summary of Findings

### Medicare's Positive Impact is Undeniable
Medicare eligibility and enrollment at age 65 significantly improve healthcare access and reduce financial strain from medical bills, particularly for low-income older adults, demonstrating its crucial role in supporting this demographic. More needs to be done to expand it despite coming defunding.

### Affordability Challenges
Despite these benefits, aging adults, particularly those nearing or in retirement, still face significant affordability challenges due to fixed incomes and the high cost of health insurance options like COBRA. This financial strain often leads to difficult choices regarding healthcare.

### The Medicare Coverage Gap
As aging adults near retirement, there is a specific and critical gap in health insurance coverage that exists for individuals who retire or have to leave work before becoming eligible for Medicare at age 65. This period can leave many vulnerable to high healthcare costs or without adequate insurance.

### Strategies for Bridging the Gap
Various and sometimes detrimental strategies exist to bridge the healthcare coverage gap for aging adults, including exploring marketplace plans, spousal coverage, or specific bridge insurance products, though these often come with their own cost considerations.

### Post-Medicare Affordability Issues
Even after becoming Medicare-eligible, affordability remains a top concern due to premiums, deductibles, co-pays, and the need for supplemental plans. Subsidies may not fully alleviate the financial burden for all beneficiaries and many need to decrease their income and assets to be eligible. For aging adults, affordability is a major cause of disparity no matter the coverage type of insurance coverage or time they have it in their lives.

### Delayed Care Due to Cost
A significant number of aging adults therefore delay or forgo necessary medical care, prescription drugs, or mental health services due to concerns about affordability and inadequate insurance coverage, leading to potentially worse health outcomes.

### Complex Health Needs
Aging adults often cannot afford to have lapses in care becuase they experience complex and compounding health needs, including chronic diseases and multiple co-morbidities, which necessitate comprehensive and continuous healthcare access. Thus, they experience more frequent and often more expensive health care costs, along with navigating the system's complexity for their compounding and degenerative conditions.

### Addressing Social Determinants for Vulnerable Populations
Even with insurance coverage being equal, there are still disparities that are caused by the social determinats of health. For example, low-income adults, who often bear a higher burden of chronic disease, face disproportionate financial barriers to healthcare and struggle to afford prescription drugs. To actually address the many disparities older adults face in their insurance and healthcare journey, any interventions must be multifaceted and look to combat not only coverage gaps, but also the social determinants that push people into them.
