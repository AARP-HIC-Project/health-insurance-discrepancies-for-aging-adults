# Environmental Forces: Rising Temperatures & Risks

Environmental forces, though often overlooked in health insurance discussions, are increasingly impacting public health and, by extension, healthcare demand and insurance resilience. Climate change, for instance, is contributing to a rise in various health issues, disproportionately affecting vulnerable populations, including older adults. 

Older adults are more susceptible to climate hazards like heat and air pollution due to pre-existing health conditions, limited mobility, and age-related changes in the body's ability to regulate temperature [^42]. Air quality degradation due to climate-related disasters such as wildfires and heatwaves exacerbates pre-existing conditions like asthma and cardiovascular disease, necessitating more frequent hospitalizations and emergency room visits among aging adults. This increased healthcare utilization drives up costs for insurance providers, which may lead to higher premiums or reduced coverage options for older adults.

Additionally, increases in average and extreme temperatures are expected to lead to more heat illnesses and deaths among older adults [^42]. For example, between 2001 and 2010, people aged 65 and older accounted for more heat-related hospitalizations than any other group[^45][^46].

Older adults may also be part of other overlapping groups vulnerable to climate change, in addition to having other [[Intersectionality|intersectional]] identities[^47]. This can increase their health risks, as some older adults can have multiple pre-existing [[Chronic Conditions|chronic conditions]] (e.g., heart disease, diabetes, dementia, cancer, etc.) that put them at greater risk. Additionally, certain immigrant groups and communities of color have a greater incidences of diseases like kidney disease, diabetes, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disorder (COPD), which can negatively compound the vulnerabilities already present in the aging population. 

Climate change can also increase outdoor air pollutants, such as ground-level ozone and particulate matter from wildfire smoke and dust from droughts, which can worsen conditions like asthma and COPD and increase the risk of heart attacks for older adults, especially those with diabetes or obesity [^42], [^43]. Warmer temperatures can increase mosquito development and biting rates, leading to a rise in vector-borne diseases like West Nile virus, which poses greater risks for older adults with compromised immune systems [^42]. Ticks, too, are expanding their range, putting more people, including adults aged 55-79 who have the highest incidence rates, at risk of contracting Lyme disease [^42].

Furthermore, natural disasters, which are becoming more frequent and intense, disrupt [[Healthcare Access|healthcare access]], damage infrastructure, and displace individuals, further complicating coverage continuity and the delivery of essential services [^44]. These events disproportionately impact already disadvantaged communities, widening the divide of social stratification and creating accumulating disadvantages for vulnerable groups [^44][^49][^50]. 

For aging adults, who may have limited mobility or live in areas with poorly maintained or aging infrastructure, natural disasters can severely hinder their ability to access necessary medical care, refill prescriptions, or maintain essential health services [^32], [^43]. Unfortunately, this area of study has often been overlooked and definitive information about the interplay and effects of climate change on health access and outcomes is still in its infacy. Even in 2011, "[t]here were no relevant follow-up articles addressing the effects of a disaster on a community with preexisting medical underservice issues or subsequent amplified health care disparities after the disaster"[^49].

Recent public health crises, such as the [[Covid-19 pandemic]], vividly exposed critical gaps in health insurance coverage and underscored the necessity of continuous enrollment provisions to prevent massive coverage losses during times of crisis [^13]. The pandemic demonstrated how external shocks can expose the fragility of existing health insurance models. 

For example, the Families First Coronavirus Response Act[^48] required states to keep people continuously enrolled in [[Medicaid]] in exchange for enhanced federal funding, a provision that ended in March 2023 [^13]. This winding process has since led to many individuals, including those in the 40-65+ age bracket, facing the risk of losing their Medicaid coverage.

While not immediately obvious as a direct driver of insurance policy, environmental forces like climate change and future public health crises like the Covid-19 pandemic will place new and unpredictable demands on the healthcare system and, by extension, on health insurance. These environmental stressors highlight the critical need for more resilient, adaptable, and comprehensive insurance frameworks that can ensure continuous coverage and equitable access to care during times of crisis. Aging populations are often more susceptible to environmental health risks and their consequences, making the integration of environmental considerations into health insurance policy a forward-looking imperative.
