# Political Forces: Cuts to Care & Coverage 

The political landscape significantly shapes the future of health insurance coverage, particularly for aging adults. The previous Trump Administration saw a period of rising uninsured rates (2017-2019), a trend attributed to specific policy actions. These included reduced funding for outreach and enrollment assistance for health insurance programs, encouragement for states to implement periodic Medicaid eligibility checks, and changes to immigration policy, such as the "public charge" rule, which deterred immigrants from utilizing public benefits out of fear of jeopardizing their immigration status [^1], [^2].

With future policies encapsulated in the current Trump Administration's "Big Beautiful Bill" (BBB), expanding on these past approaches and introducing new measures that create significant Medicaid cuts, the imposition of work requirements for enrollees, and the expiration of enhanced ACA Marketplace subsidies [^3], [^4], [^5]. These changes would directly target the public programs most relied upon by vulnerable aging adults. For instance, LGBTQ+ adults are approximately twice as likely as non-LGBTQ+ adults to rely on Medicaid as their primary insurance (13% vs. 7%), making them particularly susceptible to such cuts or new requirements [^3].

Similarly, people with disabilities heavily depend on Medicaid for comprehensive and long-term care services, which are often not covered by other insurance types [^4]. Over half of Medicaid enrollees aged 65 and older have a disability, and 43% of those aged 50-64 do [^4]. Any policy changes that curtail Medicaid funding or impose restrictive eligibility criteria would disproportionately and severely impact this population, leading to significant unmet care needs and increased financial strain.

Additionally, The BBB has introduced several provisions that increase paperwork requirements for certain groups. Specifically, the bill imposes new work and reporting requirements on Medicaid recipients whose income falls between 100% and 138% of the federal poverty level, which is approximately $32,000 to $44,000 for a family of four. Able-bodied adults between the ages of 19 and 64 will be required to prove they are working, volunteering, or attending school for at least 80 hours per month. Even those who are exempt from these requirements (i.e., including those who are pregnant, disabled or taking care of dependent children 13 or younger) will still need to demonstrate that they remain eligible for Medicaid coverage, which involves submitting additional documentation such as pay stubs [^5].

The increasing complexity of health insurance processes, particularly under potential policy shifts like those associated with a "Big Beautiful Bill," poses a severe and often overlooked threat to aging adults, especially those living with cognitive impairments such as Alzheimer's disease [^7] which has seen a +40% increase in its mortality rate since 2010.

For individuals whose lives are already made confusing by the progression of such conditions, navigating "increasingly difficult, demanding, and confusing paperwork" for health insurance becomes an insurmountable barrier, regardless of their eligibility. Policies that encourage "more frequent and stricter eligibility and citizenship checks" or impose "work requirements for able-bodied beneficiaries" [^5] introduce layers of administrative burden that are particularly detrimental.

> [!quote]+ Larry Levit | KFF executive vice president for health policy
> "The vast majority of these adults are either working or would qualify for an exemption, but millions are expected to lose coverage because they fail to navigate the reporting process... Some people would also fall through the cracks, because they will be required to renew their Medicaid coverage more frequently, every six months." [^5]

As one expert noted regarding policy changes, many are "kind of too wonky for people to talk about around the kitchen table" [^5], a sentiment amplified exponentially for those with cognitive decline. Already, "signing up was too difficult or confusing" was cited by 22.2% of uninsured nonelderly adults as a reason for lacking coverage [^1].

For the more than one in five Medicare beneficiaries in rural areas who experience cognitive impairment, these administrative hurdles are compounded by challenges in navigating the healthcare system, often requiring reliance on family or caregivers for assistance [^8], [^4]. Such policies risk not only denying essential care to those who desperately need it but also imposing immense stress on their families and caregivers, further exacerbating the profound challenges already inherent in managing Alzheimer's and other degenerative conditions.

The historical record demonstrates a clear causal link between specific political policies (e.g., reduced outreach, stricter Medicaid requirements) and rising uninsured rates, as well as widening health disparities [^1], [^2]. For example, between 2016 and 2019, the uninsured rate for the total population under age 65 increased from 10.0% to 10.9%, with Hispanic people experiencing the largest statistically significant increase in their uninsured rate (from 19.1% to 20.0%) [^2]. Furthermore, the expiration of enhanced tax credits for ACA coverage at the end of 2025 could lead to 4.2 million people joining the ranks of the uninsured, bringing the projected total to 17 million people without health insurance as early as 2026 [^5]. This would directly reverse recent coverage gains and disproportionately harm aging adults, especially those who are low-income, racial/ethnic minorities, LGBTQ+, or have disabilities, pushing them into uninsurance or underinsurance and exacerbating existing health disparities.
