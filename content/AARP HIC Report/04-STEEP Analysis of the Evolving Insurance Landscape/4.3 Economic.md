# Economic Forces: Rising Costs 

Persistent healthcare cost inflation continues to exert immense pressure on the U.S. health system, driving up premiums, deductibles, and out-of-pocket expenses, making comprehensive health insurance increasingly unaffordable for many individuals and families. 

In 2024, premiums for [[Employer-Sponsored Health Insurance (ESI)|employer-sponsored health insurance]] saw significant increases, with the average single coverage premium rising by 6% to $8,951 and family coverage increasing by 7% to $25,572[^1]. 

![[Screenshot 2025-07-13 at 11.34.32 PM.png|100%]]

These increases outpace both inflation (3.2%) and wage growth (4.5%), continuing a trend where family premiums have risen 24% over the last five years[^1]. While deductible growth has stabilized, the average deductible remains substantial at $1,787, which is 47% higher than a decade ago[^1]. This financial strain is particularly acute for employees at smaller firms, who face higher average deductibles ($2,575) compared to those at large firms ($1,538)[^1]. 

![[Screenshot 2025-07-13 at 11.35.44 PM.png|100%]]

Affordability consistently stands as the leading reason for non-elderly adults remaining [[Uninsured|uninsured]], cited by 64.2% of those surveyed in 2022[^2].

Concurrently, shifts in the employment landscape, including the rapid rise of [[The Gig Economy|the gig economy]], further erode access to stable and affordable coverage because of the inherently unpredictable nature of income for these workers, making it challenging for them to afford health insurance. Gig economy workers are typically independent contractors or freelancers who perform short-term, contingent work, and they often lack access to traditional employment benefits such as employer-sponsored health insurance, disability, and occupational health protections[^3][^4][^5]. The gig economy's growth is accompanied by an increase in part-time and freelance work arrangements, leading to a segment of the population that is neither fully employed nor unemployed but rather exists in a state of precariousness, further exacerbating the coverage gap.

> [!quote]+ Rodriguez, Fatima et al. “The Gig Economy Worker-A New Social Determinant of Health?.” 
> "A man’s employment status was a stronger predictor of his risk of dying from coronary heart disease than any of the more familiar risk factors."

This logistical features of the gig economy can lead to systemic increases in health vulnerabilities, as workers may be unable to access routine in-person primary care or afford necessary health services[^3]. For instance, a significant portion of gig workers, such as rideshare drivers, may face increased cardiovascular risks due to the inherent sedentary nature of the work along with erratic work hours and high stress levels[^3]. Furthermore, the lack of job security in these arrangements can lead to anxiety and depression, negatively impacting mental health. The [[Covid-19 pandemic]] further highlighted these vulnerabilities, as many gig workers lacked health insurance and paid sick leave, unlike traditional employees[^4].

Increased life expectancy is extending the working-years window significantly and the gig economy is providing opportunities for the Baby Boomer generation to find employment, gain valuable skills, and perform meaningful work well into their golden years without the long-term commitment many employers are unwilling to make[^6]. 

> [!quote]+ Pew Research Center
> "The majority of Baby Boomers (U.S. adults born 1946 to 1964) are still in the labor force, and the oldest among them are staying in the labor force at the highest annual rate for people their age in more than half a century. In 2018, 29% of Boomers ages 65 to 72 were working or looking for work, outpacing the labor market engagement of the Silent Generation (21%) and the Greatest Generation (19%) when they were the same age, according to a new Pew Research Center analysis of official labor force data."

![[Pasted image 20250714000124.png|100%]]

But as Baby Boomers make their way through this important transition, they once again face a layered burden of both dealing with increased rates of degenerative and [[Chronic Conditions|chronic medical conditions]], while needing to work to maintain health insurance that may not be adequate even if they are able to find it through the gig economy. 

The confluence of relentless healthcare cost inflation, the long-term erosion of employer-sponsored coverage for lower and moderate-income households, and the fundamental challenge of affordability is creating a deepening economic chasm in health insurance access. This economic pressure will inevitably force more aging adults in the 40-65+ range, particularly those in the gig economy or lower-wage professions, to struggle to afford public programs like [[Medicaid]] or into the ranks of the uninsured or [[Underinsured|underinsured]]. 

This trend intensifies the demand for robust public safety nets and more affordable [[Marketplace]] options, while simultaneously making these programs targets for cost-cutting measures in political discourse. Addressing the underlying economic drivers of uninsurance and underinsurance, rather than simply managing their symptoms, is therefore a strategic imperative for ensuring [[Health Equity|health equity]] for aging adults.
