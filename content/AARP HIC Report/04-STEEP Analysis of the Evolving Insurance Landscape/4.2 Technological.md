# Technological Forces: A Double-Edged Sword

Technological advancements are rapidly reshaping healthcare delivery and insurance models, offering both immense opportunities for improved access and efficiency, as well as significant risks of exacerbating existing disparities. [[Telehealth]], in particular, has seen a dramatic surge in adoption, with [[Medicare]] telehealth visits skyrocketing from approximately 5 million to over 53 million during the early months of the [[Covid-19 pandemic]][^1].

A striking difference emerges when examining telehealth access. Medicare beneficiaries in rural areas face significant limitations, as "[l]ower rates of telehealth offerings by rural providers may reflect differences in provider capacity and health system infrastructure challenges in rural areas relative to urban areas" [courtemancheThreeyearImpactAffordable2019](zotero://select/groups/5952825/items/U796WI5H)[2]. This highlights a core issue: rural areas often lack the infrastructure and resources to offer the same level of telehealth services as urban centers. This disparity exacerbates existing [[Healthcare Access]] challenges for rural populations[^3].

As of early 2024, 54% of Americans have engaged in [[Telehealth|telehealth]], with 38% using it for medical or mental health needs[^1]. This expansion has been critical for increasing access to care, especially in rural areas where provider shortages are common[^2], and 92% of patients using Telehealth in 2020 received care from their homes[^2]. Since then, "[r]ecent data from the Kaiser Family Foundation indicates that while utilization of telehealth has declined since 2020, utilization remains higher than pre-pandemic levels. In the last quarter of 2023, over 12.7% of Medicare beneficiaries received a telehealth service"[^4].

The limited availability of telehealth in rural areas is compounded by the fact that rural populations are typically older, less digitally literate, and may have less access to insurance, making it harder for them to access or even afford the limited healthcare options available.

However, the work to close the digital divide is not complete and the effectiveness of Telehealth is also contingent on [[Digital Literacy|digital literacy]] and reliable broadband access. The KFF found that despite the overall increase in use, there is a discrepancy that still persists between urban and rural use of these services. This is concerning for aging adults in rural areas, as they face the hardships of maintaining digital literacy in a rapidly changing technological landscape and "[e]ven seemingly basic digital skills, such as turning on a device or downloading apps, can be very demanding for older adults without prior experience" and many older adults even "have their accounts set up by a family member or even by teachers in the class because they do not know how to do it themselves"[^5].

Additionally, while deployment is improving in all geographic areas "there is still significant work to do to encourage deployment to rural areas, where more than 17% of Americans lack access, and Tribal lands, where nearly 21% of Americans lack access"[^6]. Furthermore, a 2021 report found that 56.5% of [[Medicare]] beneficiaries aged 65 and older used audio-only visits, partly because over 26% of them reported not having computer or smartphone access at home[^2]. This highlights that while Telehealth offers convenience, it can exclude those without the necessary technology or digital skills.

The increasing deployment of broadband in rural areas does have positive implications for telehealth services, as "the number of rural Americans in areas served by at least 25/3 Mbps [is] nearly 83%"[^6], and "since 2018, the number of Americans lacking access to 4G LTE mobile broadband with a median speed of 10/3 Mbps was reduced by more than 57%, including a nearly 54% decrease among rural Americans"[^6]. 

With improved access to fixed and mobile broadband, rural populations can experience better connectivity, higher speeds, and more reliable access. This enables them to engage in virtual healthcare services like video consultations, remote monitoring, and access to medical information. The narrowing [[The Digital Divide|digital divide]] empowers rural residents to overcome geographical barriers by connecting with healthcare providers and improving their overall healthcare outcomes through telehealth.

[[Artificial Intelligence|Artificial intelligence (AI)]] holds immense promise for enhancing diagnostic precision, personalizing treatment plans, and streamlining administrative processes within healthcare[^7]. AI-powered applications can analyze genetic, clinical, and lifestyle data to create more effective and individualized care plans[^7]. AI also contributes to accelerated drug discovery and enhanced predictive analytics for disease outbreaks[^7]. 

However, the ethical challenges are substantial. AI systems rely on vast amounts of data, raising concerns about patient privacy and data ownership[^8]. More critically, data used to train AI algorithms may contain biases, potentially leading to unequal treatment, misdiagnosis, or underdiagnosis of certain demographic groups, thereby perpetuating or exacerbating existing healthcare disparities[^8]. The high costs of developing and implementing cutting-edge AI technologies could inadvertently create a two-tiered system, widening the gap between those who can afford advanced care and those who cannot[^7].

Wearable technologies and remote monitoring devices, such as fitness trackers and smartwatches, are enabling more proactive and preventive care management by providing real-time health data to both patients and providers[^9]. With one in three Americans owning a wearable device, this technology offers insurers new insights for risk assessment and personalized policies[^9]. Some insurers are already incentivizing healthier behaviors with premium discounts for policyholders who meet fitness goals[^10]. 

Wearables can also detect early indicators of health issues, potentially reducing the need for expensive treatments through early intervention[^10]. However, the integration of wearable data into insurance systems presents challenges, including data accuracy, privacy concerns, and the risk of discrimination. Personalized premiums based on health metrics could unfairly penalize older individuals with pre-existing conditions or less active lifestyles, raising ethical concerns about denying or increasing insurance access based on factors beyond an individual's control[^10]. This potential for technology to widen, rather than narrow, disparities for aging adults necessitates proactive advocacy to ensure equitable access to and integration of health technologies for aging adults.

The digital divide, particularly affecting rural areas and older adults with limited digital literacy or broadband access, threatens to exacerbate existing disparities. Similarly, the ethical implications of AI bias and data privacy, alongside the potential for wearable data to lead to discriminatory insurance practices, necessitate careful oversight. Technological advancements present a complex future for aging adults, offering both transformative opportunities and significant risks. Moving forward, proactive advocacy and policy development are crucial to harness technology's benefits while mitigating its risks. While these technologies hold immense promise for enhancing healthcare access, personalizing care, and promoting preventive health, their equitable integration is paramount.
