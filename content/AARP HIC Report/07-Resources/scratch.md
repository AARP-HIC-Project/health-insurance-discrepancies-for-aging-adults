#### Future: New Barriers to Care
As the population ages, major systemic challenges threaten to deepen these disparities even further, amplifying the anxiety many older adults already feel about affording care.

While many older adults face challenges, the data shows that certain populations are disproportionately affected by barriers to healthcare access and affordability. Key factors such as geography, income, and insurance type intersect to create significant disparities. Our review of the literature highlights several key examples:

- **Racial and Geographic Disparities:** Older African Americans, particularly in the Southern U.S., face a documented shortage of primary care clinics in their communities, limiting their access to essential services. [^44]
- **Insurance-Based Barriers:** As mentioned previously, individuals enrolled in Medicaid consistently report greater difficulty finding local providers who are part of their network compared to those with private insurance. [^45]
- **Socioeconomic Vulnerability:** Older adults with more social risk factors (SRF)—such as low income, food insecurity, or unstable housing—are significantly more likely to struggle with the cost of premiums, co-pays, and prescriptions, often forcing them to delay or forgo necessary care. [^46]
- **The Digital Divide:** As healthcare increasingly pivots to digital platforms for appointments, records, and communication, it creates a new and formidable barrier. For many older adults, a "lack of access to devices, Wi-Fi or technological know-how can pose a life-threatening barrier to care".[^56] This digital exclusion risks leaving the most vulnerable behind.

In essence, a person's race, income, insurance type and where they live can dramatically impact their ability to use the health insurance they have, creating a system where the most vulnerable older adults often have the least access to stable, affordable care.

#### Insurance Coverage and Health Outcomes

Levy and Buchmueller (2025) found that health insurance coverage does reduce mortality, based on multiple high-quality studies published since 2008. The effects are strongest for adults in middle age or older and for children. For example, an IRS initiative to inform people about HealthCare.gov led to increased coverage and reduced mortality among middle-aged adults (Levy & Buchmueller, 2025). This underscores the importance of insurance coverage in improving health outcomes.

However, simply having health insurance is not enough to guarantee access to care. Josefina Flores Morales (2024) highlighted that high costs, even with insurance, create significant barriers to healthcare utilization for U.S.-born Latinx adults. This leads to a cycle of economic hardship and underuse of healthcare, with families often acting as a safety net, further straining their resources. This study emphasizes the importance of considering the broader economic and social context in which healthcare decisions are made (Morales, 2024).

# Political
The political landscape significantly shapes the future of health insurance coverage, particularly for aging adults. Discussions around a "Big Beautiful Bill" under a potential future administration, while lacking specific legislative text, can be understood through the lens of past policy directions and stated intentions. The previous Trump administration (2017-2019) saw a period of rising uninsured rates, a trend attributed to specific policy actions. These included reduced funding for outreach and enrollment assistance for health insurance programs, encouragement for states to implement periodic Medicaid eligibility checks, and changes to immigration policy, such as the "public charge" rule, which deterred immigrants from utilizing public benefits out of fear of jeopardizing their immigration status [^5], [^8].

Should future policy proposals, potentially encapsulated in a "Big Beautiful Bill," mirror these past approaches or introduce new measures, they could aim for significant Medicaid cuts, the imposition of work requirements for enrollees, and the expiration of enhanced ACA Marketplace subsidies [^8], [^9], [^11], [^13]. These changes would directly target the public programs most relied upon by vulnerable aging adults. For instance, LGBT adults are approximately twice as likely as non-LGBT adults to rely on Medicaid as their primary insurance (13% vs. 7%), making them particularly susceptible to such cuts or new requirements [^8], [^9]. Similarly, people with disabilities heavily depend on Medicaid for comprehensive and long-term care services, which are often not covered by other insurance types [^11]. Over half of Medicaid enrollees aged 65 and older have a disability, and 43% of those aged 50-64 do [^11]. Any policy changes that curtail Medicaid funding or impose restrictive eligibility criteria would disproportionately and severely impact this population, leading to significant unmet care needs and increased financial strain.

The historical record demonstrates a clear causal link between specific political policies (e.g., reduced outreach, stricter Medicaid requirements) and rising uninsured rates, as well as widening health disparities [^5], [^8]. For example, between 2016 and 2019, the uninsured rate for the total population under age 65 increased from 10.0% to 10.9%, with Hispanic people experiencing the largest statistically significant increase in their uninsured rate (from 19.1% to 20.0%) [^8]. Furthermore, the expiration of enhanced tax credits for ACA coverage at the end of 2025 could lead to 4.2 million people joining the ranks of the uninsured, bringing the projected total to 17 million people without health insurance as early as 2026 [^45]. This would directly reverse recent coverage gains and disproportionately harm aging adults, especially those who are low-income, racial/ethnic minorities, LGBTQ+, or have disabilities, pushing them into uninsurance or underinsurance and exacerbating existing health disparities.

While there are no direct statements detailing the specific impact of a "Big Beautiful Bill" on AARP, the implications of significant cuts to Medicare and Medicaid are profound for social service and advocacy organizations. If these vital public programs are curtailed, millions of aging adults would lose coverage or critical benefits, leading to a surge in demand for assistance, information, and advocacy from organizations like AARP. The political environment itself would become significantly more challenging for advocacy efforts, requiring AARP to expend greater resources and strategic effort to protect and restore vital programs [^45]. This scenario highlights the heightened imperative for AARP to maintain a robust, well-resourced, and proactive advocacy presence to mitigate the adverse effects of such policies on its members and the broader aging population.
