## Current Landscape of Health Insurance Discrepancies
> Health coverage in the U.S. is a complex patchwork of private and public insurance coverage options. In 2022, most residents had private insurance coverage, with nearly half covered by an employer-based plan . About 1 in 5 U.S. residents received coverage through the Medicaid program, the federal and state-financed comprehensive health coverage program for low-income people. Another roughly 15% of the population had health coverage from the federal Medicare program, which covers seniors and people under age 65 with long-term disabilities. About 6% of the population had private non-group insurance either purchased through the ACA Marketplace or off-market, and just over 1% of the population was covered through the military’s Tricare program. The uninsured rate for the total population was 8.0% for the year (Figure 1). (In some cases, people have multiple forms of coverage. For example, about 13 million people are enrolled in both Medicare and Medicaid and are classified in these figures as covered by Medicaid.) [^5]

The U.S. health insurance system, while showing an overall uninsured rate of approximately 8.0% in 2023, conceals significant vulnerabilities, particularly for the 18-64 age group, which had an 11.1% uninsured rate in early 2024, compared to 0.6% for those 65 and older due to Medicare eligibility [^1], [^2], [^3].

**Key Disparities:**

*   **Race/Ethnicity:** Significant disparities persist. In 2023, American Indian/Alaska Native ([[American Indian & Alaskan Native|AIAN]]) (18.7%) and Hispanic (17.9%) individuals under 65 had the highest uninsured rates, notably higher than White (6.5%) or Asian (5.8%) populations [^5]. These relative disparities have, in some cases, widened since 2010 [^5].
*   **Age:** The 50-64 age bracket is particularly vulnerable, facing higher uninsured rates than both seniors and children, often due to employment transitions or inability to afford private plans, precisely when [[Chronic Conditions]] become more prevalent [^1], [^6].
*   **Gender & LGBTQ+ Status:** In 2023, 10% of women aged 19-64 were uninsured, compared to 13% of men in the same age bracket [^7]. However, LGBTQ+ individuals are approximately twice as likely as non-LGBTQ+ adults to rely on Medicaid (13% vs. 7%) [^8], [^9], and transgender or nonbinary adults were more than twice as likely as [[Cisgender]] adults to be uninsured in 2022 [^10].
*   **Disability Status:** Medicaid is a major source of coverage for people with disabilities, covering 15 million individuals. The prevalence of disability among Medicaid enrollees rises sharply with age: 22% for ages 19-49, 43% for 50-64, and over 50% for those 65 and older [^11], [^12]. Medicaid is also the primary payer for long-term care, which Medicare and most private plans do not cover [^11].
*   **[[Socioeconomic Factors]]:** Affordability is the leading reason for nonelderly adults remaining uninsured (64.2% in 2022) [^13]. Access to employer-sponsored medical benefits varies significantly by occupation, ranging from 52% for service occupations to 94% for management, business, and financial occupations [^14].
*   **Location:** While overall uninsured rates are similar between rural and urban areas, rural Medicare beneficiaries are more likely to have lower incomes (34% earn <$20,000 vs. 27% urban) and higher rates of chronic conditions (32% with 5+ conditions vs. 25% urban) [^15], [^16]. Medicaid covers a slightly higher proportion of people in rural areas (24%) compared to urban areas (21%) [^17].

**Disparities in Services:**

*   **Dental & Vision:** Dental care is the most frequently neglected health service due to cost, with 13% of Americans skipping it for financial reasons, compared to 4-5% for general medical care [^18]. Adults aged 19-64 have the lowest dental visit rates and highest dental uninsurance (22.8%) [^18]. Adults with vision impairment also report lower overall health insurance coverage (80.6% vs. 87.6% for those without impairment) [^19].
*   **Chronic Disease Management:** Health insurance profoundly impacts chronic disease outcomes. Insured adults with diabetes show greater healthcare utilization and better A1c and cholesterol control [^20]. Uninsured individuals or those covered by Medicaid are significantly more likely to receive a cancer diagnosis at later stages (III or IV), particularly for colorectal and female breast cancer in the 50-64 age group, leading to worse survival rates [^21]. Cost-related nonadherence to prescription medication is significantly higher among older adults lacking prescription drug coverage [^22].
*   **Mental Health Care:** Insurance coverage is a key determinant in accessing [[Mental Health Services]]; insured adults with anxiety/depression are significantly more likely to receive care (64% vs. 38% for uninsured) [^23]. However, 43% of insured adults with "fair" or "poor" mental health reported not getting needed care due to cost in 2023 [^23].
