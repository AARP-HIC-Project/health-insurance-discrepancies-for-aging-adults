### Effects of Gender and Insurance on Health Outcomes

 This report examines the interplay between gender, insurance coverage, and health outcomes, drawing from recent studies and data.

#### Gender Disparities in Insurance Coverage

Gender disparities in health insurance coverage are pronounced, particularly among marginalized groups. Transgender and non-binary individuals face significant challenges in accessing adequate health insurance. According to Alnabulsi and Talbert (2024), transgender and non-binary patients with Medicaid coverage are more likely to experience denials for hormone therapy and lack in-network surgery providers. They note, "Trans adults are more likely to have public insurance than private insurance, and public insurance does not fully cover all procedures and treatment" (Alnabulsi & Talbert, 2024, p. 38). This highlights the systemic barriers that transgender individuals encounter in obtaining necessary medical care.

Similarly, Lee et al. (2022) found that gender disparities exist in health insurance coverage among older adults experiencing homelessness, with women being less likely to be insured. This vulnerability extends to postpartum women, as Seo et al. (2023) revealed that 35.6% of postpartum women in poverty lacked Medicaid or government medical assistance. These findings underscore the critical role of insurance in determining health outcomes, particularly for marginalized populations.

#### Impact of the Affordable Care Act (ACA)

The Affordable Care Act (ACA) aimed to mitigate health inequalities but fell short in fully addressing quality, affordability, and accessibility for certain marginalized groups (Alnabulsi & Talbert, 2024, p. 36). Bolibol et al. (2023) examined the impact of the ACA and the 2015 Supreme Court ruling on marriage equality on health insurance coverage and access to care for LGBT adults. They found that while disparities in insurance coverage began to decline in 2014 with the implementation of the ACA, significant disparities in access to care persisted. By 2017-19, coverage rates for LGBT adults were comparable to non-LGBT adults, but access to care remained a challenge.

#### Health Outcomes and Insurance Coverage

Increased health insurance coverage has the potential to improve health outcomes for transgender individuals. 

However, the reality is often more complex. Cohen et al. (2019) highlighted the fragmented and inconsistent nature of insurance coverage for gender-affirming surgeries in the United States. While most insurance companies covered chest masculinization (mastectomy), far fewer covered nipple-areola complex (NAC) reconstruction (20%) or chest feminization (breast augmentation) (29%). This lack of standardization creates confusion and represents a major barrier to accessing needed care.
