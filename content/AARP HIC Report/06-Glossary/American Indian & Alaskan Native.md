**Term:**  
`American Indian Alaskan Native`

**Definition:** 
American Indian Alaskan Native (often abbreviated as AI/AN or AIAN) is a racial and ethnic classification used in the United States to describe individuals who have origins in any of the original peoples of North and South America (including Central America) and who maintain tribal affiliation or community attachment. This broad category encompasses hundreds of distinct sovereign tribal nations, each with unique cultures, languages, and traditions.

**Etymology:**  
The term "American Indian" originated from European explorers' mistaken belief that they had reached India. "Alaskan Native" was added later to specifically include the indigenous peoples of Alaska (e.g., Inuit, Yup'ik, Aleut, Athabascan, Tlingit, Haida, Tsimshian), who are culturally and historically distinct from the indigenous peoples of the contiguous United States. The combined term "American Indian and Alaska Native" (AI/AN) is primarily a U.S. governmental and demographic classification, formalized in the late 20th century for statistical and policy purposes.

**Usage Notes:**  
-   **Common misconceptions to avoid:**
    *   **Monolithic Group:** It is crucial to avoid the misconception that "American Indian Alaskan Native" represents a single, homogenous group. This category encompasses hundreds of distinct sovereign tribal nations, each with unique cultures, languages, histories, and governance structures.
    *   **Historical Only:** Indigenous peoples are not solely figures of the past; they are contemporary, vibrant communities with ongoing cultural practices, political structures, and contributions to modern society.
    *   **Blood Quantum:** While some tribal nations use blood quantum for enrollment, it is a complex and often controversial concept, and not all individuals identifying as AI/AN meet specific tribal enrollment criteria. The federal definition for statistical purposes is broader.
    *   **"Native American" vs. "American Indian Alaskan Native":** While "Native American" is often used interchangeably, "American Indian and Alaska Native" is the specific term used by the U.S. government for statistical and programmatic purposes. Many individuals and tribes prefer "Indigenous Peoples," "First Nations" (in Canada), or their specific tribal affiliation.
-   **Specialized meanings in different fields:**
    *   **Government/Policy:** Used as a demographic category for federal programs, funding allocations, and treaty obligations (e.g., Indian Health Service, Bureau of Indian Affairs).
    *   **Healthcare:** Essential for understanding [[Health Disparities]] and providing [[Culturally Competent Care]]. The Indian Health Service (IHS) provides healthcare services to federally recognized American Indians and Alaska Natives.
    *   **Research:** Used in epidemiological and social science research to analyze health outcomes, [[Socioeconomic Status (SES)|socioeconomic status]], and other indicators specific to this population group.

**How this concept affects insurance coverage for older adults:**
    *   Older adults who are American Indian or Alaska Native may have unique access to healthcare services through the Indian Health Service (IHS), tribal health programs, or urban Indian health programs. These services are often considered a "payer of last resort" or a supplement to other insurance.
    *   **Coordination with Medicare/Medicaid:** For AI/AN elders, IHS and tribal health services often coordinate with Medicare (Parts A, B, D) and Medicaid. Medicare covers services not available through IHS, and IHS may cover deductibles, co-pays, or services not covered by Medicare. Medicaid can also be a primary payer for eligible AI/AN individuals, with IHS serving as a supplemental provider.
    *   **Special Enrollment Periods:** AI/AN individuals may qualify for special enrollment periods for Medicare or Marketplace plans, and certain cost-sharing reductions or exemptions.
    *   **Ethical and Societal Implications:** The provision of healthcare services to AI/AN populations stems from historical treaties and trust responsibilities of the U.S. government. However, chronic underfunding of IHS has led to significant [[Health Disparities]], including lower life expectancy and higher rates of chronic diseases among AI/AN elders compared to the general population. This highlights the ethical imperative for adequate funding and culturally appropriate care to address historical injustices and achieve [[Health Equity]].

**How this concept affects insurance coverage in general:**
    *   Beyond older adults, the AI/AN status impacts insurance for all ages due to the existence of the Indian Health Service (IHS) and tribal health programs. These programs are not insurance in the traditional sense but provide direct healthcare services.
    *   **Affordable Care Act (ACA) Provisions:** The ACA includes specific provisions for AI/AN individuals, such as exemptions from the individual mandate (when it was in effect), special enrollment periods for Marketplace plans, and enhanced cost-sharing reductions for those with lower incomes.
    *   **Medicaid Expansion:** AI/AN individuals are often disproportionately affected by state decisions regarding Medicaid expansion, as many live in rural areas with limited access to care.
    *   **Ethical and Societal Implications:** The unique relationship between the U.S. government and tribal nations means that [[Healthcare Access]] for AI/AN individuals is a matter of treaty obligation and sovereign rights, not merely a social welfare program. Inadequate insurance coverage or access to quality care for AI/AN populations reflects systemic inequities and the ongoing impact of historical trauma and colonization, leading to significant health disparities across the lifespan.

**Related Terms:**  
- **Synonyms:** Indigenous Peoples (broader, international), Native Americans (common informal usage), First Nations (primarily Canada), Aboriginal Peoples (primarily Australia).
